# Speaker Notes — Chapter 8: MNH Resource Allocation

## Overview
Alright, welcome to Chapter 8. Last chapter we built the decision-making toolkit — influence diagrams, expected value, the value of information. Today we scale all of that up. We're going from one country and three strategies to ten countries, hundreds of millions of dollars, and decisions where the cost of getting it wrong is measured in lives. This is resource allocation under deep uncertainty, and it's one of the hardest problems in global health.

## Slide: Learning Objectives
So here's what we're building toward today. Five objectives, and they escalate in complexity. *(pause)* First, framing multi-country allocation as a decision under uncertainty — that's the foundation. Then we build actual probability distributions over the key unknowns. Third, we learn sequential decision-making — commit some money, observe what happens, then scale. Fourth, we grapple with how stakeholders respond to your allocation — do governments step up or step back when you invest? And finally, we use Monte Carlo simulation to compare strategies with real confidence intervals. Every one of these maps to something a program analyst actually does during a funding cycle.

## Slide: The Rideshare Problem
Okay, let's start with something familiar. Remember the rideshare case from the textbook? A ride-sharing company has to decide how many drivers to put in San Francisco versus New York. They don't know how big each market is, they don't know how competitors will respond, and they don't know what the regulators will do. *(pause)* Sound familiar? It should. Because structurally, this is exactly the problem an MNH program faces when allocating funding across countries. Look at the core tradeoff on the right — concentrate in one city for a higher payoff if you're right, or spread across cities for lower variance. That's the same tradeoff we're going to wrestle with all chapter.

## Slide: From the Rideshare Case to the MNH Program
Now look at this mapping table. Drivers across cities becomes funding across countries. Market demand uncertainty becomes baseline burden uncertainty. Competitor legal response? That maps to government co-financing commitment. Revenue maximization becomes lives saved maximization. *(pause)* These aren't just cute analogies. The actual analytical tools — influence diagrams, expected value, decision trees, sensitivity analysis — all of them transfer directly. We built them for the rideshare case, and they work without modification for MNH allocation. Same math, different stakes.

## Slide: The Multi-Country Allocation Problem
Let's get into the real problem.

## Slide: The MNH Program Portfolio
So here's what we're actually dealing with. Look at this table. The MNH program has pooled donor funding committed over the program timeline to reduce maternal and neonatal mortality across sub-Saharan Africa. Ten countries. Hundreds of thousands of lives on the line. *(pause)* Country A alone accounts for 45,000 lives — that's driven by both a high neonatal mortality rate of 28.1 per thousand and a large birth cohort. Countries enter in different cohorts, and notice Country J at the bottom — that's still TBD pending the next funding cycle. We'll come back to that one later. The distribution of targets already reflects some analytical reasoning, but here's the core question: given all the uncertainty we face, how should the actual funding be split?

## Slide: What Makes This Hard
Three things make this allocation problem genuinely hard. *(pause)* First, look at the left box — baseline burden. This tells you the potential for impact. Country A has the largest target because of both high NMR and large birth cohort. That's your ceiling. *(pause)* Now look at the green box — health system capacity. Can the country actually absorb the investment? Country H has great systems but smaller targets. Country I has weak infrastructure but desperate need. *(pause)* And then there's the orange box at the bottom — government readiness. Will the government maintain its co-financing commitments? Will it sustain reforms? Will it absorb programs after the initiative exits? This is the most uncertain variable and the one with the largest impact on long-run outcomes. You can do everything right on the program side, and if the government doesn't follow through, it all unravels.

## Slide: Sources of Uncertainty
Let me be more specific about what we don't know. Look at this table — five major uncertainties. Government co-financing affects the long run. Intervention coverage affects how many lives you save right now. Workforce retention determines whether your capacity investments stick. Political stability is a binary risk — if things go sideways, it derails everything. And other donor behavior changes the marginal value of your funding. *(pause)* Now read the red callout. This is the fundamental challenge: we have to commit funds *before* we resolve these uncertainties. So the question isn't "what's the right allocation?" The question is: what allocation is robust to what we don't know?

## Slide: The Allocation Decision Space
Okay, let's formalize the decision. The program chooses an allocation vector — how many dollars go to each of the ten countries, and they have to sum to the total budget. *(pause)* Now, there are infinite ways to split the money, but we can think about three archetype strategies. Look at the three boxes. Concentrate means putting 70% or more into the top three countries by expected lives saved — Country A, Country B, Country C. High expected value, but high variance. Spread means roughly equal across all ten. Lower expected value, lower variance. Tiered means weighting by burden times capacity with floors and ceilings — the balanced approach. *(pause)* Look at the callout at the bottom. This is the rideshare problem at scale. Instead of two cities, we have ten countries. Instead of drivers, we have the program's total budget. Same analytical approach.

## Slide: Modeling Uncertainty
Now let's build the probability models.

## Slide: Government Co-Financing Scenarios
Of all the uncertainties we listed, government co-financing is the big one. So let's model it explicitly. *(pause)* Three scenarios. High — 40% probability — the government meets or exceeds its commitments, absorbs programs fully. You get 100% of your target. Medium — 50% probability — partial commitment, some programs sustained. You get 60% of target. Low — 10% probability — the government reneges, programs collapse after the initiative exits. You're down to 25%. *(pause)* Now look at the green box. If you do the expected value math — 0.4 times 1 plus 0.5 times 0.6 plus 0.1 times 0.25 — you get 72.5%. That's your base case: roughly three-quarters of the target. That's consistent with what we see in large-scale public health programs historically. But here's the thing — this is an average. Country-specific probabilities vary a lot.

## Slide: Country-Specific Probability Profiles
And here's where it gets interesting. Look at this table — the risk profiles are very different across countries. *(pause)* Country H has a 60% chance of high co-financing and 83.3% expected achievement. It's the safest bet you have. But look at its target from the earlier table — only 8,500 lives. Smallest in the portfolio. Now look at Country I — only 20% chance of high co-financing, 55.8% expected achievement. Much riskier. But it has one of the highest neonatal mortality rates. *(pause)* See the pattern in the orange callout? The safest countries have the smallest targets. The highest-burden countries carry the most uncertainty. You cannot optimize expected value and minimize risk at the same time. That's the core allocation tradeoff.

## Slide: Decision Tree for a Single Country
Let's walk through the expected value calculation for a single country. Take Country A. *(pause)* We allocate the full budget to Country A. There's a 40% chance of high co-financing — 45,000 lives. A 45% chance of medium — 27,000 lives. A 15% chance of low — 11,250 lives. Multiply and add: 18,000 plus 12,150 plus 1,688 equals 31,838. *(pause)* Okay, great. But read the callout at the bottom. That number is meaningless in isolation. The real question is: would those same dollars save more lives if we sent them to Country C or Country B instead? We need to compare expected lives per dollar across countries, not just total lives in one country.

## Slide: Scenario Analysis: Best / Base / Worst
Now let's zoom out to the full portfolio — all ten countries. *(pause)* Best case, where every country achieves high co-financing: about 185,000 lives. Full target. Base case, using the country-specific probability mix: about 131,000 lives, or 70.7% of target. Worst case, where every country lands at low co-financing: about 46,000 lives. That's 25%. *(pause)* Let that worst case sink in. Three-quarters of the target gone. That's what motivates portfolio diversification. When the downside is that severe, you cannot afford to concentrate all your bets.

## Slide: Sequential Decision Making
Okay, here's the good news.

## Slide: The Multi-Stage Structure
The program doesn't have to commit everything on day one. Look at this flow. Stage 1: commit seed funding. Observe: wait 12 to 18 months for pilot results. Stage 2: based on what you learned, either scale up or reallocate. *(pause)* This is exactly the rideshare structure from the textbook. The ride-sharing company can test a market before going all-in. The MNH program can run pilots before committing full allocations. The value of this sequential approach depends on how informative the pilot signal is. If the pilot tells you something useful, the optionality is worth a lot. If it's just noise, you've delayed for nothing.

## Slide: Rolling Back the Decision Tree
Let's do the backward induction for Country A's two-stage decision. *(pause)* After a promising pilot — and there's a 35% chance of that — the updated probability of high co-financing jumps to 0.70, you scale up, and expected lives are 38,200. After a mixed signal — 45% chance — you proceed moderately, 25,300 lives. After a negative signal — 20% chance — you redirect the funds elsewhere, only 6,400 lives from the initial seed. *(pause)* Now roll it back. EV of the sequential approach equals 0.35 times 38,200 plus 0.45 times 25,300 plus 0.20 times 6,400. That gives you 26,035 lives. *(pause)* Wait — that's *less* than the 31,838 from committing everything upfront. Why would we do this? Because less money is committed overall. And look at the downside: the worst case under the sequential approach is dramatically better. You're trading expected value for risk reduction.

## Slide: The Value of Waiting vs. the Cost of Delay
This is the slide I want you to really sit with, because it's what makes health allocation fundamentally different from business portfolio optimization. *(pause)* Look at the left column — the value of waiting. Better information leads to better allocation. You avoid dumping large sums into a country with low co-financing. The pilot provides an imperfect but valuable signal. Portfolio variance goes down. All good things. *(pause)* Now look at the right column — the cost of delay. Lives lost during the pilot period. Every month of delay means preventable neonatal deaths that are actually happening. An 18-month pilot delay puts tens of thousands of potential lives at stake. Staff and political momentum may dissipate. *(pause)* Read the key takeaway at the bottom. The cost of delay is measured in lives. Sequential decision-making is valuable, but it is not free. This tension between learning and acting — that's what makes this problem so hard and so different from deciding how to allocate rideshare drivers.

## Slide: MNH Application: 2-Stage Decision for Country J
Let's apply this to a concrete case. Country J is planned for the next phase of funding. Program leadership has to decide now whether to begin preparatory investments. *(pause)* Stage 1: invest in a health system strengthening pilot in three subnational regions. Observe government co-financing behavior, workforce absorption, political stability. Then Stage 2: based on pilot results, commit the bigger allocation from the subsequent funding cycle. *(pause)* Look at the numbers. Pilot leads to a promising signal — 30% chance — you go full scale, 24,000 expected lives. Mixed signal — 40% — partial scale, 9,000 lives. Negative signal — 30% — you exit, 1,000 lives from the seed investment only. EV of the sequential approach is 11,200 lives. *(pause)* Compare that to the no-pilot, full-commit option: 12,500 lives. So you lose 1,300 expected lives. But look at the orange callout — the pilot saves significant resources in expected cost. And those freed resources? They can be deployed in higher-confidence countries. The freed capital is the real win here.

## Slide: Stakeholder Response
Now let's add another layer of complexity.

## Slide: Crowding Out vs. Crowding In
So far we've treated the program's investment as if it exists in a vacuum. It doesn't. Other actors — governments, other donors — respond to what you do. *(pause)* Crowding out is when your investment causes others to reduce their spending. "The program's got it covered, so we'll redirect our money elsewhere." Crowding in is the opposite — your investment catalyzes additional spending. "This sector is getting results, let's invest more." *(pause)* Look at the two boxes. In the crowding-out scenario on the left, the program invests in Country B, the government redirects funds to other priorities, and you end up with only 63% of expected lives saved. Net new investment is reduced. In the crowding-in scenario on the right, the government adds matching funds, another multilateral co-invests, and you get 150% of expected lives saved. Same program dollars, dramatically different impact.

## Slide: Modeling Other Donors
Let's put numbers on this. Other donors — multilateral agencies, bilateral agencies — respond to the program's allocation with a probability distribution. *(pause)* 30% chance they redirect away — "it's covered" — and you get a 0.7x multiplier on your investment. 45% chance they maintain existing programs — 1.0x multiplier. 25% chance they match or expand — 1.4x multiplier. *(pause)* Do the math: 0.30 times 0.7 plus 0.45 times 1.0 plus 0.25 times 1.4 equals 1.01. So on average, crowding effects roughly cancel out. But read the orange callout — it's the variance that matters. In 30% of cases, your dollar buys only 70 cents of new investment. That's a real problem. Strategic coordination with other donors can shift these probabilities in your favor, which is why donor coordination matters so much.

## Slide: Preview: Game Theory (Chapter 9)
Now, we've been treating these stakeholder responses as chance nodes — random variables with probability distributions. But here's the thing: governments and donors aren't rolling dice. They're making strategic choices based on their own objectives. *(pause)* That's fundamentally a game theory problem, and it's what Chapter 9 is about. Nash equilibrium, commitment devices, signaling, mechanism design — those are the tools for navigating multi-actor strategic interactions. *(pause)* The green callout sets our boundary for today. For now, stakeholder behavior is a chance node in our decision model. Next chapter, we upgrade it to a strategic variable with its own decision logic.

## Slide: MNH Application: 3-Country Allocation
Okay, let's put the full framework to work.

## Slide: The 3-Country Problem
Here's a simplified but concrete allocation problem. Program leadership has a fixed budget to distribute across Country A, Country C, and Country B — the three largest target countries. *(pause)* Look at the table. Country A: 45,000 lives target, 70.8% expected achievement, 610 lives per million dollars. Country C: 27,000 target, 76.5% expected achievement, 356 lives per million. Country B: 38,000 target, 72.8% expected achievement, 462 lives per million. *(pause)* Now, the naive approach — just look at lives per dollar and send almost everything to Country A. Right? Highest efficiency? Read the orange callout. That ignores risk — Country A has more uncertainty than the others. And it ignores diminishing returns — marginal impact decreases with scale. Pouring all the money into one country isn't as smart as it looks.

## Slide: Influence Diagram for 3-Country Allocation
Let me walk you through the influence diagram. One decision node — the allocation vector across Country A, Country C, and Country B. That feeds into coverage nodes for each country. Coverage is influenced by both the allocation and the uncertain factors — government co-financing and donor response, shown in amber. Everything flows into the objective node: total lives saved. *(pause)* The key feature here is that the uncertain variables are country-specific. Each country has its own co-financing probability and its own donor response distribution. That means the optimal allocation depends on the joint uncertainty structure — how the risks are correlated across countries.

## Slide: Comparing Strategies
Alright, here's where the rubber meets the road. Three strategies for this three-country sub-portfolio. *(pause)* Concentrate: 70% to Country A, 15% each to Country C and Country B. Highest expected value at 57,400 lives, but also the highest standard deviation at 14,500. A bad outcome in Country A would be catastrophic. *(pause)* Spread: equal thirds. Lowest expected value at 53,200, but lowest standard deviation at 9,800. *(pause)* Tiered: 45% to Country A, 23% to Country C, 32% to Country B. Expected value of 56,100 with standard deviation of 11,500. *(pause)* Look at the green callout. The Tiered strategy sacrifices modest expected lives compared to Concentrate but reduces the standard deviation by about 20%. If you've taken a finance course, you'll recognize this — it's efficient frontier logic from portfolio theory. You're getting most of the upside with meaningfully less risk.

## Slide: Sensitivity to Government Co-Financing
Now here's a practical question: at what point does the co-financing risk actually change which strategy we pick? *(pause)* Let p equal the probability of high co-financing specifically for Country A. As p goes up, the Concentrate strategy's expected value rises steeply — because most of the money is riding on Country A. The Tiered strategy is less sensitive because the funds are more diversified. *(pause)* Look at the table. At P(High) of 0.20, Tiered wins — 49,700 versus 48,000. At P(High) of 0.40, Concentrate pulls ahead — 57,400 versus 56,100. *(pause)* The crossover point is around 0.35. So if program leadership believes there's more than a 35% chance Country A's government will fully commit, concentrating becomes optimal in pure expected value terms. But the risk-adjusted answer depends on how much downside leadership is willing to tolerate.

## Slide: Recommendation with Confidence Intervals
Here's our recommendation. *(pause)* The Tiered strategy: Country A gets 45%, Country C gets 23%, Country B gets 32%. Expected lives saved: approximately 56,100, with a 95% confidence interval from about 34,900 to 75,600. *(pause)* Four reasons this is the right call. First, robustness — Tiered performs within 3% of Concentrate when things go well, and substantially better when things go badly. Second, portfolio diversification — three countries with different risk profiles means less chance of catastrophic shortfall. Third, political feasibility — extreme concentration raises equity concerns among participating governments. And fourth, adaptive capacity — this allocation preserves the option to reallocate in subsequent cycles based on what you actually observe. You're keeping your options open.

## Slide: R Code Workshop

## Slide: R Block 1: Monte Carlo Simulation
Alright, open up your R environment. We're going to simulate outcomes for each allocation strategy using 10,000 Monte Carlo draws. The simulation incorporates government co-financing scenarios and country-specific uncertainty. Look at the simulate_outcomes function — for each simulation draw, it randomly selects a co-financing scenario for each country based on that country's probability profile, applies the multiplier, adds some noise to the effectiveness estimate, and tallies up total lives saved. We define our three strategies as allocation vectors and we're ready to go.

## Slide: R Block 2: Visualize Strategy Outcomes
Now let's see what these simulations look like. We run all three strategies through the simulator and create violin plots with embedded box plots. *(pause)* This is where the risk-return tradeoff becomes visually obvious. You should see the Concentrate distribution is wider — fatter tails on both ends. The Spread distribution is tighter but shifted left. And the Tiered distribution sits in that sweet spot. The shape of these distributions tells you more than any single number could.

## Slide: R Block 3: Optimal Allocation via Grid Search
Here's where we let the computer do the heavy lifting. We search over the allocation space in 5% increments — every possible way to split the budget across three countries in 5-point steps. For each allocation, we run 2,000 simulations and record the expected value, the standard deviation, and the 5th percentile — that worst-case metric. *(pause)* Then we find two things: the allocation that maximizes expected value, and the allocation that maximizes the worst case — the maximin allocation. Notice how those two objectives lead to different answers. That tension between maximizing the average and protecting against the downside is the whole story of this chapter.

## Slide: R Block 4: Sensitivity Analysis
Last code block. We vary Country A's co-financing probability from 0.1 all the way up to 0.8 and plot how it affects the expected value of each strategy. *(pause)* You should see the Concentrate line rising steeply — it's very sensitive to this parameter. The Spread line is nearly flat. And the Tiered line is in between. Where the Concentrate line crosses above the Tiered line — that's your crossover point, around 0.35. This is the kind of analysis that actually informs real allocation decisions: which parameter matters most, and how confident do you need to be before switching strategies?

## Slide: Key Takeaways
Let me pull this together. Six lessons from today. *(pause)* One — portfolio thinking applies to public health. Diversification reduces catastrophic shortfall risk. Two — sequential decisions add value by resolving uncertainty, but delay costs lives. That tension never goes away. Three — government co-financing is the dominant uncertainty. Small shifts in the probability of high co-financing change the optimal strategy. Four — stakeholder response can amplify or undermine your impact. Crowding out versus crowding in is not trivial. Five — Monte Carlo simulation in R makes it possible to quantify confidence intervals and compare strategies rigorously, not just with point estimates. *(pause)* And six — the cost of delay is measured in lives. That's what distinguishes health allocation from commercial portfolio optimization. In business, delay costs money. In global health, delay costs lives.
