Core Insight: In Chapter 4 we learned to build quantitative models forward — specifying how causes produce effects. In Chapter 5 we run the model backwards — observing outcomes and inferring what caused them. This is the heart of situational analysis.

The Manager’s Inference Problem

Running the model backwards

From Forward to Backward Reasoning

Situational Analysis: The process of observing outcomes and using a quantitative causal model to infer the most likely state of unobserved causes. We run the model “backwards” — from effects to causes.

Chapter 4 (Forward)

Given causes, predict effects:

“If staffing is low and equipment unavailable, what is the probability of high NMR?”

Direction: Parents → Children

Chapter 5 (Backward)

Given effects, infer causes:

“We observe high NMR in a district — what does this tell us about staffing and equipment?”

Direction: Children → Parents

Marginal Probabilities

What is the overall probability of an outcome?

What Is a Marginal Probability?

Marginal Probability: The probability of a variable computed by summing out (marginalizing over) all other variables in the joint distribution. It tells us the overall likelihood of an event, averaging across all possible parent combinations.

\[P(X) = \sum_{\text{all other variables}} P(X, Y_1, Y_2, \ldots, Y_n)\]

Intuition: “What is the overall probability that NMR is High in a randomly selected district?” We don’t know the district’s staffing or equipment — so we average over all possible staffing/equipment combinations, weighted by how likely each combination is.

Marginal P(NMR=High): The Calculation

We sum over all paths through the network:

\[P(\text{NMR=High}) = \sum_{s,e,q} P(S=s) \cdot P(E=e) \cdot P(Q=q \mid S=s, E=e) \cdot P(\text{NMR=High} \mid Q=q)\]

S E P(S) P(E) P(Q=Good) P(NMR=H | Good) P(NMR=H | Poor) Contribution
Adeq Avail 0.40 0.35 0.90 0.15 0.70 0.029
Adeq Unavail 0.40 0.65 0.60 0.15 0.70 0.097
Low Avail 0.60 0.35 0.50 0.15 0.70 0.089
Low Unavail 0.60 0.65 0.15 0.15 0.70 0.240

P(NMR=High) = 0.455

Without any evidence, there is a 45.5% baseline probability that a randomly selected district has high neonatal mortality.

Evidence and Updating

How observations change our beliefs

What Does “Evidence” Mean?

Evidence (Observation): Learning the actual state of one or more variables in the model. Mathematically, this means conditioning the joint distribution on the observed values.

Before evidence (prior):

\(P(\text{Staffing=Low}) = 0.60\)

This is our general belief across all districts.

After evidence (posterior):

\(P(\text{Staffing=Low} \mid \text{NMR=High}) = \; ?\)

Observing high NMR in a specific district — what does this tell us about staffing?

Bayes’ Rule: The Engine of Updating

\[P(\text{Staffing=Low} \mid \text{NMR=High}) = \frac{P(\text{NMR=High} \mid \text{Staffing=Low}) \cdot P(\text{Staffing=Low})}{P(\text{NMR=High})}\]

Step 1: Compute \(P(\text{NMR=High} \mid \text{Staffing=Low})\) by marginalizing over Equipment and Quality:

\[P(\text{NMR=H} \mid S=\text{Low}) = \sum_{e,q} P(E=e) \cdot P(Q=q \mid S=\text{Low}, E=e) \cdot P(\text{NMR=H} \mid Q=q) = 0.549\]

Step 2: Apply Bayes’ rule:

\[P(S=\text{Low} \mid \text{NMR=H}) = \frac{0.549 \times 0.60}{0.455} = 0.724\]

Belief shift: Observing high NMR increases our belief that staffing is low from 60% → 72.4%. The evidence makes the “low staffing” explanation more likely.

The General Pattern of Updating

Belief Updating Rule

Evidence that is more likely under hypothesis A than hypothesis B increases our belief in A relative to B. This is the likelihood ratio at work.

Variable Prior Posterior (given NMR=High) Shift
P(Staffing=Low) 0.60 0.724 +0.124 ↑
P(Staffing=Adequate) 0.40 0.276 −0.124 ↓
P(Equipment=Unavailable) 0.65 0.741 +0.091 ↑
P(Equipment=Available) 0.35 0.259 −0.091 ↓

Both parent nodes shift toward “bad” states when we observe a bad outcome. But staffing shifts more — suggesting it has a stronger causal connection to NMR in this model.

Inference in Serial Structures

A → B → C: Chains and blocking

Serial Structure: The Chain

Serial Structure (Chain): A → B → C. Information flows from A through B to C. A and C are marginally dependent but become conditionally independent given B.

MNH Example:

Training
(T)
→ [+]
Competency
(C)
→ [−]
Mortality
(M)
  • Training causes competency, which causes (lower) mortality
  • If we observe competency directly, training gives us no additional information about mortality
  • Conditioning on the intermediate node blocks the chain

Serial: CPTs and Priors

Conditional probability tables for the serial chain:

Training P(C=High) P(C=Low)
Completed 0.80 0.20
Not Completed 0.30 0.70
Competency P(M=Low) P(M=High)
High 0.85 0.15
Low 0.35 0.65

Prior: \(P(T=\text{Completed}) = 0.50\), \(P(T=\text{Not Completed}) = 0.50\)

Marginal competency:

\[P(C=\text{High}) = 0.50 \times 0.80 + 0.50 \times 0.30 = 0.55\]

Serial: Worked Bayesian Calculation

Scenario: We observe that Mortality is High. What can we infer about Training?

Step 1: Marginal \(P(M=\text{High})\):

\[P(M=H) = P(C=H) \cdot P(M=H \mid C=H) + P(C=L) \cdot P(M=H \mid C=L)\] \[= 0.55 \times 0.15 + 0.45 \times 0.65 = 0.375\]

Step 2: \(P(M=H \mid T=\text{Completed})\):

\[= 0.80 \times 0.15 + 0.20 \times 0.65 = 0.250\]

Step 3: Bayes’ rule:

\[P(T=\text{Completed} \mid M=H) = \frac{0.250 \times 0.50}{0.375} = 0.333\]

Result: Observing high mortality shifts our belief about training from 50% → 33.3% completed. High mortality is evidence against training having been completed.

Serial: Blocking by Conditioning

Now suppose we also know that Competency is Low. Does Training still matter?

\[P(T=\text{Completed} \mid M=H, C=\text{Low}) = P(T=\text{Completed} \mid C=\text{Low})\]

Since \(T \perp\!\!\!\perp M \mid C\) in a serial chain, mortality gives no additional information once competency is known.

\[P(T=\text{Comp} \mid C=\text{Low}) = \frac{P(C=\text{Low} \mid T=\text{Comp}) \cdot P(T=\text{Comp})}{P(C=\text{Low})} = \frac{0.20 \times 0.50}{0.45} = 0.222\]

Blocking in serial chains: Once we observe the intermediate node (Competency), the downstream outcome (Mortality) tells us nothing new about the upstream cause (Training). The chain is blocked.

Inference in Diverging Structures

A ← B → C: Common cause correlation

Diverging Structure: The Fork

Diverging Structure (Fork): A ← B → C. B is a common cause of both A and C. A and C are marginally dependent (correlated through B) but become conditionally independent given B.

MNH Example:

PPH
Detection
← [+]
Workforce
Quality
→ [+]
CPAP
Usage
  • Workforce Quality drives both PPH detection and CPAP usage
  • PPH detection and CPAP usage are correlated — districts good at one tend to be good at the other
  • This correlation is not causal — improving PPH detection does not improve CPAP usage
  • Conditioning on Quality blocks the spurious correlation

Diverging: CPTs and Setup

CPTs for the diverging structure:

Quality P(PPH_Det=Good) P(PPH_Det=Poor)
Good 0.90 0.10
Poor 0.35 0.65
Quality P(CPAP=Proper) P(CPAP=Improper)
Good 0.85 0.15
Poor 0.25 0.75

Prior: \(P(Q=\text{Good}) = 0.45\) (from marginal calculation in our model)

Marginals:

\[P(\text{PPH=Good}) = 0.45 \times 0.90 + 0.55 \times 0.35 = 0.598\] \[P(\text{CPAP=Proper}) = 0.45 \times 0.85 + 0.55 \times 0.25 = 0.520\]

Diverging: Inference from One Child to Another

Scenario: We observe PPH Detection is Poor in a district. What does this tell us about CPAP Usage?

Step 1: Update belief about Quality given PPH=Poor:

\[P(Q=\text{Good} \mid \text{PPH=Poor}) = \frac{0.10 \times 0.45}{0.402} = 0.112\]

Step 2: Use updated Quality to predict CPAP:

\[P(\text{CPAP=Proper} \mid \text{PPH=Poor}) = 0.112 \times 0.85 + 0.888 \times 0.25 = 0.317\]

Result: Observing poor PPH detection drops our belief that CPAP usage is proper from 52.0% → 31.7%. Information flows from one effect to another through the common cause — even though there is no direct causal link.

Diverging: Blocking by Conditioning on Common Cause

Now suppose we know that Quality is Good. Does PPH detection still tell us about CPAP?

\[P(\text{CPAP=Proper} \mid \text{PPH=Poor}, Q=\text{Good}) = P(\text{CPAP=Proper} \mid Q=\text{Good}) = 0.85\]

Since \(\text{PPH} \perp\!\!\!\perp \text{CPAP} \mid Q\) in a diverging structure, PPH detection is irrelevant once we know Quality.

Blocking in diverging structures: Once we observe the common cause (Quality), knowing one effect (PPH Detection) tells us nothing about the other effect (CPAP Usage). The fork is blocked.

Management implication: If you can directly measure workforce quality, you don’t need to infer it from proxy indicators. Direct measurement blocks the indirect reasoning path and gives sharper diagnoses.

Inference in Converging Structures

A → B ← C: The explaining away phenomenon

Converging Structure: The Collider

Converging Structure (Collider): A → B ← C. B is a common effect of A and C. Unlike serial and diverging structures, A and C are marginally independent but become dependent when we condition on B. This is the most counterintuitive pattern in causal reasoning.

MNH Example:

Staffing
(S)
→ [+]
Workforce
Quality (Q)
← [+]
Equipment
(E)

The counterintuitive part: Staffing and Equipment are independent causes — knowing one tells you nothing about the other. But once we observe their common effect (Quality), they become dependent. Learning about one changes our belief about the other.

Explaining Away: The Core Idea

The Explaining Away Intuition

Scenario: You observe that a district has Good workforce quality. This is surprising — it could be because staffing is adequate, OR because equipment is available, OR both.

Now you learn that staffing is Low. What happens to your belief about equipment?

Answer: Equipment must be Available — because something has to explain the good quality, and staffing can’t be the explanation. Equipment “explains away” what staffing cannot.

This is called “explaining away”: When two independent causes compete to explain the same effect, confirming one cause makes the other less likely (and disconfirming one makes the other more likely).

Converging: Worked Calculation

CPT for Quality (from our model):

Staffing Equipment P(Q=Good) P(Q=Poor)
Adequate Available 0.90 0.10
Adequate Unavailable 0.60 0.40
Low Available 0.50 0.50
Low Unavailable 0.15 0.85

Step 1: Observe Q=Good. Update belief about Equipment:

\[P(E=\text{Avail} \mid Q=\text{Good}) = \frac{P(Q=\text{Good} \mid E=\text{Avail}) \cdot P(E=\text{Avail})}{P(Q=\text{Good})}\]

\[P(Q=\text{Good} \mid E=\text{Avail}) = 0.40 \times 0.90 + 0.60 \times 0.50 = 0.660\]

\[P(Q=\text{Good}) = 0.35 \times 0.660 + 0.65 \times (0.40 \times 0.60 + 0.60 \times 0.15) = 0.35 \times 0.660 + 0.65 \times 0.330 = 0.446\]

\[P(E=\text{Avail} \mid Q=\text{Good}) = \frac{0.660 \times 0.35}{0.446} = 0.518\]

Converging: The Explaining Away Effect

Step 2: Now also observe S=Low. Update belief about Equipment:

\[P(E=\text{Avail} \mid Q=\text{Good}, S=\text{Low}) = \frac{P(Q=\text{Good} \mid S=\text{Low}, E=\text{Avail}) \cdot P(E=\text{Avail})}{P(Q=\text{Good} \mid S=\text{Low})}\]

\[P(Q=\text{Good} \mid S=\text{Low}) = 0.35 \times 0.50 + 0.65 \times 0.15 = 0.273\]

\[P(E=\text{Avail} \mid Q=\text{Good}, S=\text{Low}) = \frac{0.50 \times 0.35}{0.273} = 0.642\]

Condition P(Equipment=Available)
Prior (no evidence) 0.350
Given Q=Good 0.518
Given Q=Good AND S=Low 0.642

Explaining away in action: Learning that staffing is low — when quality is good — increases our belief that equipment must be available from 51.8% to 64.2%. Equipment must be compensating for the staffing deficit.

MNH Application: District Diagnosis

A complete worked example with realistic data

The Diagnostic Scenario

Alert: District Mwanga reports a neonatal mortality rate of 35 per 1,000 live births — well above the national average of 25 per 1,000. The Beginnings Fund country director asks: What is most likely going wrong?

Available evidence:

  • NMR = High (35/1,000 vs. target of 25/1,000)
  • No direct observation of staffing, equipment, training, or quality
  • We have the national-level causal model with CPTs

Our task: Use Bayesian inference to update beliefs about all parent nodes, identify the most likely root cause, and recommend targeted investigation.

\[P(S=\text{Low} \mid \text{NMR=H}) = \frac{\sum_{e,t,q} P(S=\text{Low}) \cdot P(E=e) \cdot P(T=t) \cdot P(Q=q \mid S=\text{Low},e,t) \cdot P(\text{NMR=H} \mid q)}{P(\text{NMR=H})}\]

Staffing has the largest posterior shift — it moves from 60% to 71%. This suggests staffing is the most informative variable to investigate first in District Mwanga.

R Code Workshop

Bayesian inference with bnlearn

R Block 1: Build a BN with CPTs

library(bnlearn)
library(gRain)

# Define the DAG structure
dag <- model2network("[Staffing][Equipment][Training][Quality|Staffing:Equipment:Training][NMR|Quality]")

# Define CPTs
cpt_s <- matrix(c(0.40, 0.60), ncol = 2,
                dimnames = list(NULL, c("Adequate", "Low")))

cpt_e <- matrix(c(0.35, 0.65), ncol = 2,
                dimnames = list(NULL, c("Available", "Unavailable")))

cpt_t <- matrix(c(0.50, 0.50), ncol = 2,
                dimnames = list(NULL, c("Completed", "Not_Completed")))

# Quality CPT: P(Quality | Staffing, Equipment, Training)
cpt_q <- array(
  c(0.95, 0.05,  # S=Adeq, E=Avail, T=Comp
    0.80, 0.20,  # S=Adeq, E=Avail, T=Not
    0.65, 0.35,  # S=Adeq, E=Unavail, T=Comp
    0.45, 0.55,  # S=Adeq, E=Unavail, T=Not
    0.55, 0.45,  # S=Low, E=Avail, T=Comp
    0.35, 0.65,  # S=Low, E=Avail, T=Not
    0.20, 0.80,  # S=Low, E=Unavail, T=Comp
    0.08, 0.92), # S=Low, E=Unavail, T=Not
  dim = c(2, 2, 2, 2),
  dimnames = list(Quality = c("Good", "Poor"),
                  Staffing = c("Adequate", "Low"),
                  Equipment = c("Available", "Unavailable"),
                  Training = c("Completed", "Not_Completed")))

# NMR CPT: P(NMR | Quality)
cpt_nmr <- matrix(c(0.85, 0.15, 0.30, 0.70), ncol = 2,
                  dimnames = list(NMR = c("Low", "High"),
                                  Quality = c("Good", "Poor")))

# Fit the BN with custom CPTs
bn_fit <- custom.fit(dag, list(
  Staffing = cpt_s, Equipment = cpt_e, Training = cpt_t,
  Quality = cpt_q, NMR = cpt_nmr))

R Block 2: Query with Evidence

# --- Marginal probability (no evidence) ---

        event = (NMR == "High"),
        evidence = TRUE,

# ≈ 0.455

cpquery(bn_fit,
        event = (Staffing == "Low"),
        evidence = (NMR == "High"),
        n = 1e6)
# ≈ 0.71

# --- Evidence on a root node ---
# What is P(NMR = High | Staffing = Low)?
cpquery(bn_fit,
        event = (NMR == "High"),
        evidence = (Staffing == "Low"),
        n = 1e6)
# ≈ 0.55

# --- Multiple pieces of evidence ---
# P(NMR = High | Staffing = Low, Equipment = Unavailable)
cpquery(bn_fit,
        event = (NMR == "High"),
        evidence = (Staffing == "Low" & Equipment == "Unavailable"),
        n = 1e6)
# ≈ 0.63

R Block 3: Compare Posterior vs. Prior

# Function to compute posteriors for all root nodes given NMR evidence
compute_posteriors <- function(bn, nmr_state, n_samples = 1e6) {
  data.frame(
    Variable = c("Staffing=Low", "Equipment=Unavail", "Training=Not_Comp"),
    Prior = c(0.60, 0.65, 0.50),
    Posterior = c(
      cpquery(bn, (Staffing == "Low"), (NMR == nmr_state), n = n_samples),
      cpquery(bn, (Equipment == "Unavailable"), (NMR == nmr_state), n = n_samples),
      cpquery(bn, (Training == "Not_Completed"), (NMR == nmr_state), n = n_samples)
    )
  )
}

# Posteriors given NMR=High (District Mwanga)
post_high <- compute_posteriors(bn_fit, "High")
post_high$Shift <- post_high$Posterior - post_high$Prior
print(post_high)

# Posteriors given NMR=Low (District Songea)
post_low <- compute_posteriors(bn_fit, "Low")
post_low$Shift <- post_low$Posterior - post_low$Prior
print(post_low)

R Block 5: Full District Diagnosis Workflow

# ---- Full diagnosis workflow for a district ----

diagnose_district <- function(bn, evidence_list, n_samples = 1e6) {
  # Build evidence string
  ev_expr <- paste(names(evidence_list), "==",
                   paste0('"', evidence_list, '"'), collapse = " & ")

  # Query each root node
  results <- data.frame(
    Node = c("Staffing", "Equipment", "Training"),
    Bad_State = c("Low", "Unavailable", "Not_Completed"),
    Prior = c(0.60, 0.65, 0.50)
  )

  results$Posterior <- sapply(1:nrow(results), function(i) {
    cpquery(bn,
            event = eval(parse(text = paste0(results$Node[i], ' == "',
                                              results$Bad_State[i], '"'))),
            evidence = eval(parse(text = ev_expr)),
            n = n_samples)
  })

  results$Shift <- results$Posterior - results$Prior
  results$Priority <- rank(-abs(results$Shift))
  results[order(results$Priority), ]
}

# Diagnose District Mwanga (NMR=High)
cat("=== District Mwanga Diagnosis ===\n")
mwanga <- diagnose_district(bn_fit, list(NMR = "High"))
print(mwanga)

# Diagnose with additional evidence (NMR=High, Quality=Poor)
cat("\n=== With additional evidence: Quality=Poor ===\n")
mwanga_deep <- diagnose_district(bn_fit,
                                  list(NMR = "High", Quality = "Poor"))
print(mwanga_deep)

Key Takeaways

Marginal probabilities tell us baseline expectations. The marginal P(NMR=High) = 45.5% is the starting point before any district-specific evidence.

Evidence updates beliefs via Bayes’ rule. Observing NMR=High shifts all root causes toward their “bad” states — with staffing showing the largest shift.

The three structures determine information flow: serial and diverging chains are blocked by conditioning on the middle node; converging chains are activated by it.

Explaining away is the most counterintuitive phenomenon. In a collider, observing the common effect makes independent causes compete — confirming one reduces belief in the other.
