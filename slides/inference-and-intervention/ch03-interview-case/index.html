The Consulting Mindset

How consultants build causal models from interviews

The Iterative Process

Consulting Causal Modeling: An iterative cycle of hypothesis → interview → model revision → deeper questions → refinement.

  • Start simple: Begin with the most stripped-down model possible
  • Walk back through causes: What causes the outcome? What causes those causes?
  • Identify decision variables: What can the client actually control?
  • Sign the links: Does increasing X increase or decrease Y?
  • Test with interviews: Does the model match what stakeholders report?
  • Iterate: Add complexity only when you actually need it

Key Principles from the Book

1. Focus on Significant Drivers

Not all variables matter equally. Identify the ones with the largest causal effects on the objective.

2. Seek Sources of Common Problems

Look for common causes that explain multiple symptoms. These are high-leverage intervention points.

3. Ask Specific Questions

General questions yield vague answers. Use the model to generate specific hypotheses to test.

4. Bring It All Together

The final model should tell a coherent causal story that links decisions to outcomes.

The Case Setup

Country X’s MNH program is underperforming

The Client Brief

Client: The Meridian Health Alliance Investment Committee

Engagement: Assess why Country X’s MNH program has not achieved projected mortality reductions despite $50M in investment over 2 years.

Key Facts Provided:

Metric Target (Year 2) Actual (Year 2)
Neonatal Mortality Rate 22/1,000 28/1,000
Maternal Mortality Ratio 280/100K 340/100K
Intervention Coverage 70% 42%
Funds Disbursed $50M $48M

The puzzle: The money has been spent, but outcomes haven’t improved as projected. Why?

Initial Situational Assessment

Following the book’s approach, start by mapping what we know:

Walk back from the objective:

  • What causes NMR reduction? → Quality of care during labor, delivery, and neonatal period
  • What causes quality of care? → Intervention coverage (PPH bundle, CPAP, iKMC)
  • What causes intervention coverage? → Investment in products, workforce, systems
  • Investment has been made ($48M disbursed) → So something between investment and coverage is broken

Hypothesis: The bottleneck is not in funding but in the transmission mechanism from investment to outcomes.

Interview Round 1: Ministry of Health

The supply-side view

MOH Director Interview

What Questions to Ask Next?

The model suggests the bottleneck is in the transmission from inputs to utilization:

  • Why aren’t trained health workers applying their training? → Need to interview facility directors
  • Why is equipment procured but not used? → Need to understand facility-level barriers
  • Is staffing the binding constraint? → Need data on staff:patient ratios by facility

The model guides the next interview. Without it, we would be asking unfocused questions. The model generates specific, testable hypotheses.

Interview Round 2: Facility Directors

The front-line reality

Hospital Director Interviews

Key findings from visiting 5 district hospitals:

Finding 1: Staff Attrition

“We trained 40 nurses on CPAP last year. Only 18 are still here. The rest transferred, emigrated, or left the profession.” Staff retention is a critical missing variable.

Finding 2: Equipment Maintenance

“The CPAP machines arrived 8 months ago. Two of our four units are already broken. We have no biomedical technician and spare parts take 3 months to arrive.” Equipment maintenance breaks the chain.

Finding 3: Referral Failures

“When a mother develops eclampsia at a health center, it takes 4-6 hours to arrange transport to our hospital. Many arrive too late.” Referral system gaps are an independent cause of mortality.

Critical discovery: Staff retention and equipment maintenance are common causes (diverging triplets) that affect multiple downstream variables. These are high-leverage intervention points that the initial model completely missed.

Identifying Triplet Structures

The expanded model reveals all three triplet types from Chapter 2:

Serial (Chain)

Training → Competency → Coverage

Conditioning on competency blocks the flow from training to coverage

Diverging (Fork)

Competency ← Retention → Utilization

Staff retention is a common cause of both low competency (trained staff leave) and low utilization (not enough staff to operate equipment)

Converging (Collider)

Staffing → Quality ← Equipment

If we observe Quality is low AND Equipment is available, we can infer Staffing must be inadequate (explaining away)

Interview Round 3: Community Health Workers

The demand side

Community Health Worker Interviews

New perspective: the demand side of MNH services

Finding 4: Transport Barriers

“Many mothers in rural areas cannot reach the hospital in time. The nearest facility with a neonatal unit is 3 hours away by foot.” Geographic access limits who benefits from improved facility quality.

Finding 5: Cultural Practices

“Some families prefer traditional birth attendants. They only come to the hospital when complications are severe — by then it’s often too late.” Health-seeking behavior mediates the effect of facility quality.

Finding 6: Out-of-Pocket Costs

“Even though services are officially free, families pay for transport, food, and supplies during their stay. Many can’t afford it.” Financial barriers reduce effective demand.

Updating the Model: Round 3

Adding demand-side nodes: Geographic Access, Health-Seeking Behavior, Financial Barriers

The complete model now has 12 nodes organized in three layers:

Supply Side (from Rounds 1-2)

  • Investment → Training → Competency
  • Investment → Equipment → Utilization
  • Staff Retention affects both
  • Equipment Maintenance affects utilization
  • Referral System affects access

Demand Side (Round 3)

  • Geographic Access → Facility Births
  • Health-Seeking Behavior → Facility Births
  • Financial Barriers → Facility Births
  • Facility Births × Quality → NMR

The Complete Causal Model

The final model tells a coherent story: Investment flows through three channels (people, products, systems), but is undermined by staff attrition, equipment breakdown, and referral failures on the supply side — and by geographic, cultural, and financial barriers on the demand side. Both supply and demand must work together to reduce NMR.

Prioritizing Recommendations

Using the model to prioritize interventions:

High Priority (Root causes with many downstream effects):

1 Staff Retention Programs

Root cause affecting both competency AND utilization. Invest in housing allowances, career paths, bonding agreements.

2 Equipment Maintenance System

Deploy biomedical technicians and establish spare parts supply chains. Equipment without maintenance is wasted investment.

Medium Priority:

3 Emergency Referral Network

Independent pathway to mortality reduction. Partner with m-mama for transport coordination.

4 Demand-Side Interventions

Community health workers, transport subsidies, culturally-sensitive outreach to increase facility births.

Why Root Nodes Matter

Root Node: A node without any parents in the causal model. Root nodes are ultimate causes — intervening on them has the broadest downstream effects.

In our model, the key root nodes are:

  • Investment allocation (decision variable — we control this)
  • Staff retention (not currently addressed — new intervention needed)
  • Equipment maintenance (not currently addressed — new intervention needed)
  • Geographic access (structural constraint — requires referral systems)

The model reveals that Meridian Health Alliance invested in the right inputs (training, equipment) but missed critical transmission mechanisms (retention, maintenance). Redirecting 15-20% of the budget to these mechanisms could unblock the entire chain.

R: Identifying What to Measure

# What must we control for to estimate the causal effect
# of investment on NMR?
adjustmentSets(round3,
               exposure = "Investment",
               outcome = "NMR")

# What are the implied conditional independencies?
# These are testable predictions of the model
impliedConditionalIndependencies(round3)

# What are all paths from Investment to NMR?
paths(round3,
      from = "Investment",
      to = "NMR")

Practical Value: The adjustmentSets() function tells us which variables we must measure and control for in our evaluation. The impliedConditionalIndependencies() generate testable predictions — if the data violates them, our model is wrong.

Key Takeaways

Build models iteratively. Start simple and add complexity as interviews reveal new variables and mechanisms.

Root nodes are leverage points. Staff retention and equipment maintenance emerged as critical bottlenecks that the initial investment plan overlooked.

The model guides the analysis. Each round’s model tells you what questions to ask next, what data to collect, and where the bottlenecks are.
