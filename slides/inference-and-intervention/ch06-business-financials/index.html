Cost-Effectiveness in MNH

Why naive country comparisons mislead

The Key Metric: Cost Per Life Saved

Cost Per Life Saved (CPLS): The total program expenditure divided by the number of additional lives saved attributable to the intervention. Lower CPLS means greater efficiency.

Why CPLS matters for the Beginnings Fund:

  • The Fund has $525 million to deploy across 10 countries
  • Each country has different baseline mortality, health system capacity, and cost structures
  • Naive comparisons of CPLS across countries can be deeply misleading
  • A country with high CPLS may actually be the best investment when you account for confounders

The core problem: Comparing CPLS without adjusting for confounders (baseline severity, health system strength, population density) is like comparing apples to oranges – and can lead to catastrophically wrong allocation decisions.

Simpson’s Paradox

When aggregated data reverses the truth

Definition: Simpson’s Paradox

Simpson’s Paradox: A trend that appears in aggregated data reverses or disappears when the data is disaggregated by a confounding variable. The paradox arises when a lurking variable is associated with both the treatment and the outcome.

Why it matters:

  • Decisions based on aggregated data can be exactly wrong
  • The paradox is not a statistical curiosity – it has led to real-world policy failures
  • Resolution requires causal reasoning, not just statistical adjustment
  • The key question: Should you condition on a variable, or not? The answer depends on the causal structure.

Classic Example: UC Berkeley Admissions

The Famous Case (1973)

UC Berkeley was sued for gender bias in graduate admissions:

Applicants Admitted Rate
Men 8,442 3,738 44%

Women appeared to be discriminated against overall.

But when broken down by department:

Within most departments, women were admitted at higher rates. The paradox arose because women applied disproportionately to competitive departments with low admission rates. Department choice was the confounder.

The DAG That Generates Simpson’s Paradox

Simpson’s Paradox arises whenever a confounder creates a fork structure: a variable that is a common cause of both the treatment assignment and the outcome.

Resolution: Condition on (stratify by) the confounder. The stratified estimates give the correct causal effect.

Critical rule: Always condition on confounders (common causes of treatment and outcome). Never condition on mediators (variables on the causal path between treatment and outcome). Conditioning on a mediator blocks the causal effect you are trying to estimate.

Key Insight: Confounders vs. Mediators

Condition on CONFOUNDERS

Confounder is a common cause:

Treatment \(\leftarrow\) Confounder \(\rightarrow\) Outcome

Conditioning removes the spurious association and reveals the true causal effect.

Always stratify by confounders

Do NOT condition on MEDIATORS

Mediator is on the causal path:

Treatment \(\rightarrow\) Mediator \(\rightarrow\) Outcome

Conditioning blocks the very causal pathway you want to measure.

Never stratify by mediators

MNH Example

  • Confounder: Baseline severity (condition on it – YES)
  • Mediator: Quality of care improvement (do NOT condition on it – blocking the effect of investment on mortality)
  • Getting this wrong leads to Simpson’s Paradox or underestimation of treatment effects

The Prosecutor’s Fallacy

Confusing the direction of conditional probability

Definition: The Prosecutor’s Fallacy

The Prosecutor’s Fallacy: Confusing \(P(\text{Evidence} \mid \text{Hypothesis})\) with \(P(\text{Hypothesis} \mid \text{Evidence})\). That is, mistaking the probability of observing the evidence given that a hypothesis is true for the probability that the hypothesis is true given the evidence.

The classic courtroom version:

  • A DNA match occurs in 1 in 1,000,000 people
  • Prosecutor argues: “There is only a 1-in-a-million chance the defendant is innocent!”
  • But in a city of 5 million people, 5 people match the DNA
  • \(P(\text{match} \mid \text{innocent}) = 0.0001\%\) but \(P(\text{innocent} \mid \text{match})\) could be \(\frac{4}{5} = 80\%\)

The fallacy: The probability of the evidence given innocence is NOT the probability of innocence given the evidence. You need Bayes’ rule and the base rate to convert one into the other.

MNH Example: Preeclampsia Screening

The Diagnostic Test

A screening test for preeclampsia in pregnant women has the following performance:

  • Sensitivity: 90% – \(P(\text{positive test} \mid \text{has preeclampsia}) = 0.90\)
  • Specificity: 85% – \(P(\text{negative test} \mid \text{no preeclampsia}) = 0.85\)
  • Prevalence: 5% – \(P(\text{preeclampsia}) = 0.05\)

The Prosecutor’s Fallacy in action:

A health worker sees a positive test result and tells the patient: “The test is 90% accurate, so there is a 90% chance you have preeclampsia.”

This is WRONG. The 90% is \(P(\text{positive} \mid \text{disease})\), not \(P(\text{disease} \mid \text{positive})\). To find the latter, we need Bayes’ rule.

Bayes’ Rule Calculation

\[P(\text{disease} \mid \text{positive}) = \frac{P(\text{positive} \mid \text{disease}) \times P(\text{disease})}{P(\text{positive})}\]

Step 1: Compute \(P(\text{positive})\) using the law of total probability:

\[P(\text{positive}) = P(\text{pos} \mid \text{disease}) \times P(\text{disease}) + P(\text{pos} \mid \text{no disease}) \times P(\text{no disease})\] \[= (0.90)(0.05) + (0.15)(0.95) = 0.045 + 0.1425 = 0.1875\]

Step 2: Apply Bayes’ rule:

\[P(\text{disease} \mid \text{positive}) = \frac{(0.90)(0.05)}{0.1875} = \frac{0.045}{0.1875} \approx 0.24\]

A positive test means only a 24% chance of preeclampsia – not 90%! The low base rate (5% prevalence) means most positive tests are false positives.

Visualizing the Fallacy

Consider 10,000 pregnant women screened:

Has Preeclampsia (500) No Preeclampsia (9,500) Total
Test Positive 450 (true pos) 1,425 (false pos) 1,875
Test Negative 50 (false neg) 8,075 (true neg) 8,125

Of the 1,875 positive tests, only 450 (24%) actually have preeclampsia.

The key insight is that prevalence (base rate) is a prior that must be combined with the likelihood (test accuracy) to produce the posterior (probability of disease given positive test). Ignoring the base rate is the essence of the Prosecutor’s Fallacy.

The PPH bundle reduces mortality in BOTH strata. The aggregated data is misleading because the bundle is deployed disproportionately in weak-system countries (where baseline mortality is higher). Health system strength is the confounder.

Policy Implication: The Next $50M

Decision: The Beginnings Fund has an additional $50M to allocate. Where should it go?

Without causal reasoning:

  • Allocate to Rwanda and Ghana (lowest raw CPLS)
  • Expected lives saved: ~8,200
  • Ignores diminishing returns in strong systems

With causal reasoning:

  • Allocate to Ethiopia and Tanzania (highest marginal impact after confounder adjustment)
  • Expected lives saved: ~14,600
  • Accounts for unmet need, baseline severity, and system capacity

The causal approach saves an additional 6,400 lives with the same budget.

This is not hypothetical. Misallocation of global health funds due to Simpson’s Paradox-style reasoning has been documented in HIV/AIDS, malaria, and vaccination programs. Causal thinking is not academic – it is life-or-death.

R Code Workshop

Simulating and analyzing Simpson’s Paradox and the Prosecutor’s Fallacy

R Block 1: Simulating Simpson’s Paradox

library(tidyverse)

set.seed(42)

# Simulate MNH data exhibiting Simpson's Paradox
# Confounder: country_group (high-burden vs low-burden)
n <- 2000

mnh_data <- tibble(
  # Confounder: country group determines both treatment and baseline risk
 country_group = sample(c("High-burden", "Low-burden"), n, replace = TRUE,
                         prob = c(0.6, 0.4)),
  # Treatment assignment depends on country group (confounder)
  cpap = case_when(
    country_group == "High-burden" ~ rbinom(n, 1, 0.75),
    country_group == "Low-burden"  ~ rbinom(n, 1, 0.25)
  ),
  # Outcome depends on BOTH country group and treatment
  # CPAP has a positive causal effect (+10pp), but high-burden has lower baseline
  survival_prob = case_when(
    country_group == "High-burden" & cpap == 1 ~ 0.68,
    country_group == "High-burden" & cpap == 0 ~ 0.61,
    country_group == "Low-burden"  & cpap == 1 ~ 0.85,
    country_group == "Low-burden"  & cpap == 0 ~ 0.82
  ),
  survived = rbinom(n, 1, survival_prob)
)

# Aggregated result (misleading)
mnh_data |>
  group_by(cpap) |>
  summarise(survival_rate = mean(survived), n = n(), .groups = "drop")

# Disaggregated result (correct)
mnh_data |>
  group_by(country_group, cpap) |>
  summarise(survival_rate = mean(survived), n = n(), .groups = "drop")

R Block 3: Naive vs. Confounder-Adjusted Regression

# Naive regression: ignores confounder
naive_model <- lm(survived ~ cpap, data = mnh_data)
summary(naive_model)
# Expected: CPAP coefficient is NEGATIVE (misleading)

# Confounder-adjusted regression: includes country_group
adjusted_model <- lm(survived ~ cpap + country_group, data = mnh_data)
summary(adjusted_model)
# Expected: CPAP coefficient is POSITIVE (correct)

# Compare coefficients side by side
tibble(
  Model = c("Naive (no confounder)", "Adjusted (with confounder)"),
  CPAP_Coefficient = c(coef(naive_model)["cpap"],
                       coef(adjusted_model)["cpap"]),
  Interpretation = c("CPAP appears harmful",
                     "CPAP is beneficial (+7pp)")
)

Key lesson: The naive model estimates a negative effect of CPAP on survival. The adjusted model, which conditions on the confounder (country group), correctly estimates a positive effect. Omitted variable bias is the statistical signature of Simpson’s Paradox.

R Block 4: Bayesian Network with bnlearn

library(bnlearn)

# Prepare data for bnlearn (factors required)
bn_data <- mnh_data |>
  mutate(
    country_group = factor(country_group),
    cpap = factor(cpap, labels = c("No", "Yes")),
    survived = factor(survived, labels = c("Died", "Survived"))
  ) |>
  select(country_group, cpap, survived) |>
  as.data.frame()

# Define the causal structure (DAG)
dag <- model2network("[country_group][cpap|country_group][survived|cpap:country_group]")
plot(dag)

# Fit the BN with maximum likelihood estimates
fitted_bn <- bn.fit(dag, bn_data)

# Query: P(survived | do(cpap = Yes)) using causal reasoning
# Marginalizing over country_group (the back-door adjustment)
cpquery(fitted_bn,
        event = (survived == "Survived"),
        evidence = (cpap == "Yes"),
        n = 100000)

# Compare: P(survived | cpap = Yes, country_group = High-burden)
cpquery(fitted_bn,
        event = (survived == "Survived"),
        evidence = (cpap == "Yes" & country_group == "High-burden"),
        n = 100000)

# The BN correctly handles the conditioning automatically

The Bayesian network encodes the causal structure. Queries on the fitted BN respect the DAG and give correct causal estimates, avoiding Simpson’s Paradox by construction.

R Block 5: Prosecutor’s Fallacy Calculator

# Bayes' rule calculator for diagnostic tests
bayes_diagnostic <- function(sensitivity, specificity, prevalence) {
  # P(positive | disease) = sensitivity
  # P(negative | no disease) = specificity
  # P(positive | no disease) = 1 - specificity (false positive rate)
  # P(disease) = prevalence

  p_pos_given_disease    <- sensitivity
  p_pos_given_no_disease <- 1 - specificity
  p_disease              <- prevalence
  p_no_disease           <- 1 - prevalence

  # Total probability of a positive test
  p_positive <- (p_pos_given_disease * p_disease) +
                (p_pos_given_no_disease * p_no_disease)

  # Bayes' rule: P(disease | positive)
  p_disease_given_pos <- (p_pos_given_disease * p_disease) / p_positive

  # Return results
  tibble(
    Metric = c("Sensitivity", "Specificity", "Prevalence",
               "P(Positive Test)", "P(Disease | Positive) [PPV]",
               "Prosecutor's Fallacy Would Claim"),
    Value = c(
      scales::percent(sensitivity, accuracy = 0.1),
      scales::percent(specificity, accuracy = 0.1),
      scales::percent(prevalence, accuracy = 0.1),
      scales::percent(p_positive, accuracy = 0.1),
      scales::percent(p_disease_given_pos, accuracy = 0.1),
      scales::percent(sensitivity, accuracy = 0.1)
    )
  )
}

# Preeclampsia screening example
bayes_diagnostic(
  sensitivity = 0.90,
  specificity = 0.85,
  prevalence  = 0.05
)

# What if prevalence is higher (e.g., high-risk population)?
bayes_diagnostic(
  sensitivity = 0.90,
  specificity = 0.85,
  prevalence  = 0.20
)

Note how the PPV changes dramatically with prevalence: At 5% prevalence, PPV is ~24%. At 20% prevalence, PPV rises to ~60%. The same test, the same sensitivity and specificity – but the meaning of a positive result depends entirely on the base rate. This is why the Prosecutor’s Fallacy is so dangerous.

Key Takeaways

Simpson’s Paradox arises when a confounder creates a fork structure in the DAG. Aggregated data can reverse the true causal effect. Always stratify by confounders, never by mediators.

The Prosecutor’s Fallacy confuses \(P(E \mid H)\) with \(P(H \mid E)\). Base rates matter. Use Bayes’ rule to compute the correct posterior probability.

In MNH cost-effectiveness, naive cross-country comparisons mislead because baseline severity and health system strength confound the treatment-outcome relationship. Causal adjustment can change which countries and interventions are prioritized – and save thousands of additional lives.
