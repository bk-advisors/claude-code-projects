# Speaker Notes — Chapter 6: Modeling MNH Cost-Effectiveness

## Overview
Today we're going to look at two analytical traps that can completely wreck real-world decisions — Simpson's Paradox and the Prosecutor's Fallacy. These are places where ignoring causal structure doesn't just give you a slightly wrong answer, it gives you the *opposite* of the right answer. And in the context of MNH cost-effectiveness, that means you'd literally be sending money to the wrong countries. So this chapter is where the theoretical toolkit we've been building across Chapters 1 through 5 starts saving lives.

## Slide: Learning Objectives
Alright, five objectives for today, and every one of them maps to something you'd actually have to do as a program analyst. By the end of this session, you'll be able to spot Simpson's Paradox hiding in aggregated program data. You'll know how to avoid the Prosecutor's Fallacy using Bayes' rule. You'll be able to apply both of those insights to cost-effectiveness analysis. We'll simulate these paradoxes in R so you can see them emerge from data. And ultimately, you'll be equipped to make sound resource-allocation recommendations — the kind where getting it right or wrong is the difference between thousands of lives saved or lost.

## Slide: The Slimtree Publishing Case
So let's start with the textbook connection. Ryall and Bramson use a publishing company's profit-and-loss statement to show how making causal structure explicit reveals hidden assumptions. And the parallel to what we're doing is surprisingly direct. *(pause)* Think about it — a P&L spreadsheet is already an implicit causal model. Inputs minus costs equals profit. Right? Well, a cost-per-life-saved calculation is also an implicit causal model — cost divided by lives saved. The difference is that nobody treats a CPLS number like a causal model. They treat it like a fact. And that's where things go wrong. Making the structure explicit — using a DAG — is what reveals where the reasoning can break down.

## Slide: Transition: From Profit to Lives Saved
Look at the two columns here. On the left, Slimtree Publishing — maximize profit, revenue minus costs, levers are pricing and volume. On the right, MNH cost-effectiveness — maximize lives saved per dollar, cost divided by lives saved, levers are intervention choice and country allocation. *(pause)* The paradox works the same way in both domains. A product line that looks unprofitable overall but is profitable in every segment? That maps directly to an intervention that looks ineffective overall but is effective in every country group. Same causal structure, different domain. The orange callout at the bottom nails it: the same causal reasoning transfers across these very different contexts.

## Slide: The Key Metric: Cost Per Life Saved
Okay, let's talk about the number that drives most of the big decisions in MNH programming: Cost Per Life Saved, or CPLS. It's straightforward — total program expenditure divided by the number of additional lives saved. Lower is better. More lives per dollar. *(pause)* But here's the problem, and it's right there in the red callout. Comparing CPLS across countries without adjusting for confounders is like comparing apples to oranges. A country with a high CPLS might actually be the *best* investment once you account for baseline severity, health system capacity, and population density. The raw number is misleading without causal context. And that's what this whole chapter is about.

## Slide: Lives Saved Targets by Country
Let's ground this in some real scale. The program targets nearly 300,000 lives saved across nine countries. Country A alone accounts for about 26.5% of that target — roughly 78,000 lives. That's driven by population size and high baseline neonatal mortality, not just by spending more. *(pause)* Now, notice that the distribution of targets already reflects some causal reasoning — larger allocations to higher-burden countries. But the key question we're going to dig into today is whether the CPLS-based comparisons that guide resource allocation are actually accounting for confounders correctly. Spoiler: often they're not.

## Slide: The Danger of Naive Rankings
This is the headline slide. This is the one I want you to really pay attention to. *(pause)* Look at the left column — the naive approach. You rank countries by raw CPLS, and Country H comes out on top at $820. Seems obvious, right? Invest everything in Country H. *(pause)* Now look at the right column. When you adjust for baseline severity and health system strength, the rankings completely reverse. Country A, which looked mediocre at $1,200 raw, becomes the most efficient at $980 adjusted. Country H's low raw CPLS wasn't telling you about superior intervention efficiency — it was telling you that Country H already has a strong health system. Investing everything there would be precisely the wrong decision. This is Simpson's Paradox applied to resource allocation, and we're going to spend the next section understanding exactly why it happens.

## Slide: Simpson's Paradox
Let's set up the formal definition before we go deeper.

## Slide: Definition: Simpson's Paradox
Here it is. Simpson's Paradox is when a trend that appears in aggregated data reverses or disappears when you disaggregate by a confounding variable. *(pause)* And the critical question — the one that separates good analysts from dangerous ones — is: should you condition on a particular variable, or not? And the answer depends on the causal structure. Is the variable a confounder? Then yes, condition on it. Is it a mediator? Then no, don't. You literally cannot answer this question without a DAG. That's why causal reasoning isn't optional here — it's the only way to resolve the paradox.

## Slide: Classic Example: UC Berkeley Admissions
Let me give you the most famous illustration of Simpson's Paradox before we apply it to MNH. In 1973, UC Berkeley was sued for gender bias in graduate admissions. And looking at the aggregate numbers, it seemed like an open-and-shut case — men were admitted at 44%, women at 35%. Clear discrimination, right? *(pause)* But then someone looked at the data department by department. And within most departments, women were actually admitted at *higher* rates than men. Look at this table — Department A, women 82% versus men 62%. Department B, women 68% versus men 63%. *(pause)* So what was going on? Women disproportionately applied to the most competitive departments — the ones with low admission rates for everyone. Department choice was the confounder. Once you stratified by department, the apparent bias vanished. Keep this example in mind, because the exact same mechanism is about to show up in our MNH data.

## Slide: MNH Example: CPAP Intervention
Now let's bring it home. Imagine you're evaluating CPAP — Continuous Positive Airway Pressure for neonatal respiratory distress — across all program countries. You look at the aggregated data and... CPAP appears to *reduce* survival. 72% survival with CPAP versus 78% without. *(pause)* That's alarming. Should we abandon CPAP? *(pause)* But now look at what happens when we disaggregate by country group. In high-burden countries — Countries A, B, and D — CPAP improves survival by 7 percentage points. In low-burden countries — Countries H, C, and E — CPAP improves survival by 3 percentage points. CPAP helps in *every single group*. The aggregated result is entirely driven by a composition effect: CPAP is used more in high-burden countries, and high-burden countries have lower baseline survival. The intervention works. The comparison was misleading.

## Slide: The Confounder: Baseline Severity
So let's make the mechanism crystal clear with a DAG. Look at the diagram. Baseline severity is sitting at the top — it's a fork, a common cause. It drives *both* CPAP usage — because high-burden countries use CPAP more frequently — *and* survival rate — because high-burden countries have lower baseline survival. *(pause)* So when you look at the aggregated data, you're seeing two things tangled together: the true positive effect of CPAP on survival, and the negative association between baseline severity and survival. The composition effect overwhelms the treatment effect. The solution? Condition on country group — stratify by baseline severity — and the confounding disappears. Now you can see the true positive effect of CPAP.

## Slide: Key Insight: Confounders vs. Mediators
Okay, I want you to really internalize this decision rule, because it comes up constantly. *(pause)* Look at the left column: condition on confounders. A confounder is a common cause of both treatment and outcome. Conditioning removes the spurious association and reveals the true causal effect. Always stratify by confounders. *(pause)* Now look at the right column: do NOT condition on mediators. A mediator is on the causal path between treatment and outcome. If you condition on it, you *block* the very causal effect you're trying to measure. Never stratify by mediators. *(pause)* Here's the MNH example at the bottom. Baseline severity is a confounder — yes, condition on it. Quality of care improvement is a mediator — if you condition on that, you'd be blocking the effect of investment on mortality. Getting this wrong causes either Simpson's Paradox on one side or underestimation of treatment effects on the other.

## Slide: The Numbers in Detail
Let me walk you through exactly how the composition effect works, because it's important to see the mechanics. *(pause)* Look at the left table — the aggregated view. A thousand patients with CPAP, a thousand without. Survival rate is 72% with CPAP, 78% without. CPAP looks worse. But now look at the right side. 80% of CPAP cases come from high-burden countries, but only 30% of non-CPAP cases do. These two groups are completely non-comparable without stratification. *(pause)* In the high-burden stratum, CPAP improves survival from 61% to 68%. In the low-burden stratum, from 82% to 85%. Positive effect in both. The aggregated numbers are meaningless because they're mixing groups with fundamentally different baselines. It's like averaging the swimming speed of fish and cats and concluding that having fins doesn't help.

## Slide: The DAG That Generates Simpson's Paradox
Let's generalize what we just saw. Simpson's Paradox arises whenever a confounder creates this fork structure — a variable that is a common cause of both the treatment assignment and the outcome. *(pause)* Look at the DAG. Confounder at the top, arrows going down to Treatment on the left and Outcome on the right. Treatment also has a direct arrow to Outcome — that's the causal effect we want to measure. But the backdoor path through the confounder creates a spurious association that can overwhelm or even reverse the true effect. *(pause)* The resolution is always the same: condition on the confounder. Stratify. The orange callout at the bottom is the single most important analytical rule in this chapter, and I'll say it again: always condition on confounders, never condition on mediators.

## Slide: The Prosecutor's Fallacy
Now let's shift to the second trap.

## Slide: Definition: The Prosecutor's Fallacy
The Prosecutor's Fallacy is about confusing two conditional probabilities that look similar but are completely different. P of Evidence given Hypothesis is not the same as P of Hypothesis given Evidence. *(pause)* The courtroom example makes it vivid. A DNA match occurs in 1 in a million people. The prosecutor stands up and says, "There's only a one-in-a-million chance the defendant is innocent!" But wait — in a city of 5 million people, 5 people match the DNA. So P of innocence given a match could be as high as 4 out of 5, or 80%. *(pause)* You need Bayes' rule and the base rate to convert one probability into the other. And this error isn't just a courtroom curiosity — it's ubiquitous in program evaluation and clinical diagnosis. Let me show you.

## Slide: MNH Example: Preeclampsia Screening
Here's how the Prosecutor's Fallacy shows up in MNH work. A screening test for preeclampsia has 90% sensitivity and 85% specificity. Prevalence in the population is 5%. *(pause)* A health worker gets a positive test result and tells the patient, "The test is 90% accurate, so there's a 90% chance you have preeclampsia." *(pause)* This is wrong. The 90% is the probability of a positive test *given* the disease — that's P of Evidence given Hypothesis. But what the patient needs to know is the probability of disease *given* a positive test — that's P of Hypothesis given Evidence. Those are not the same number. And with only 5% prevalence, most positive tests are actually false positives. To find the real number, we need Bayes' rule.

## Slide: Bayes' Rule Calculation
Let's walk through the math step by step. *(pause)* First, we need P of positive — the total probability of getting a positive test. That's the probability of a true positive plus the probability of a false positive. So: 0.90 times 0.05, plus 0.15 times 0.95. That gives us 0.045 plus 0.1425, which equals 0.1875. So about 18.75% of all women test positive. *(pause)* Now we apply Bayes' rule. P of disease given positive equals 0.045 divided by 0.1875, which is approximately 0.24. *(pause)* Let that land for a second. A positive test means only a 24% chance of preeclampsia. Not 90%. Twenty-four percent. The low base rate — just 5% prevalence — means the false positives vastly outnumber the true positives. This is why screening programs need to be evaluated with Bayes' rule, not just sensitivity and specificity.

## Slide: Visualizing the Fallacy
Let me make this even more concrete with a natural frequency table. Take 10,000 pregnant women. At 5% prevalence, 500 have preeclampsia and 9,500 don't. *(pause)* Of the 500 who have it, the test correctly identifies 450 — those are true positives. Of the 9,500 who don't have it, the test incorrectly flags 1,425 — those are false positives. So out of 1,875 total positive tests, only 450 — that's 24% — actually have the disease. The other 1,425 are healthy women getting a scare. *(pause)* Look at the DAG at the bottom. Prevalence feeds into disease status, which feeds into the test result. The prevalence — the base rate — must be combined with the likelihood to produce the posterior. Ignoring the base rate is the essence of the Prosecutor's Fallacy.

## Slide: MNH Application: Cross-Country Cost-Effectiveness
Alright, now let's put both of these lessons — Simpson's Paradox and the Prosecutor's Fallacy — to work on the real allocation problem.

## Slide: Cost-Effectiveness Data Across Countries
Here's a table showing raw CPLS for three interventions — CPAP, the PPH bundle, and workforce training — across eight countries. *(pause)* Now, what's the temptation? Pick the lowest number. Country H, PPH bundle, $720. Done, right? Invest there. *(pause)* But look at the orange callout. Country H has a baseline NMR of just 16 per 1,000. Its health system is already relatively strong. That low CPLS reflects a well-functioning health system, not necessarily a larger causal impact of the intervention. The intervention there is efficient because the system does most of the work. But there's less room for improvement. The marginal impact — the additional lives saved per additional dollar — may be much larger in Country A, even though its raw CPLS is higher.

## Slide: Naive Ranking vs. Causal Ranking
This is the policy punchline. *(pause)* Left column, red box: the naive ranking by raw CPLS. Country H PPH at $720, Country F PPH at $830, Country H CPAP at $870. Notice a pattern? It concentrates investment in countries with strong health systems and low mortality burden. *(pause)* Right column, green box: the causal ranking, adjusted for confounders. Country A PPH, Country D PPH, Country B PPH — these are the countries where the *causal effect* of each intervention is greatest. Where each additional dollar has the largest marginal impact on lives saved. *(pause)* The rankings barely overlap. And getting this wrong doesn't just waste money. It costs thousands of lives. The confounders driving the difference — baseline NMR, health system capacity, population size, existing coverage — are exactly the kinds of variables you need to condition on.

## Slide: Simpson's Paradox in Cost-Effectiveness
Here's another Simpson's Paradox example, and this one is about mortality rates directly. Look at the aggregated data on the left. The PPH bundle appears worse than standard care — 3.2% mortality versus 2.8%. Should we abandon the PPH bundle? *(pause)* Now look at the stratified data on the right. In weak health systems — Countries A, D, F, and B — the bundle reduces mortality from 5.8% to 4.1%. In strong systems — Countries H, C, E, and G — it reduces mortality from 1.9% to 1.5%. The bundle works in both strata. *(pause)* The aggregated result is misleading because the PPH bundle is deployed disproportionately in weak-system countries where baseline mortality is already higher. Health system strength is the confounder, and if you don't stratify by it, you get the wrong answer.

## Slide: Policy Implication: The Next Funding Allocation
Okay, here's where it all comes together. The program has an additional tranche of funding to allocate. Where should it go? *(pause)* Left column, red box: without causal reasoning, you'd allocate to Country H and Country E — lowest raw CPLS. Expected lives saved: about 8,200. You'd be ignoring diminishing returns in strong health systems. *(pause)* Right column, green box: with causal reasoning, you'd allocate to Country A and Country B — highest marginal impact after confounder adjustment. Expected lives saved: about 14,600. Same budget. *(pause)* That's 6,400 additional lives. With the same amount of money. Just by asking the causal question correctly. *(pause)* And the orange callout at the bottom makes a point I want to emphasize — this is not hypothetical. Misallocation of global health funds due to exactly this kind of Simpson's Paradox reasoning has been documented in HIV/AIDS, malaria, and vaccination programs. Causal thinking is not academic. It is life-or-death.

## Slide: R Code Workshop
Let's move to the coding portion.

## Slide: R Block 1: Simulating Simpson's Paradox
Here we're going to simulate MNH data that exhibits Simpson's Paradox. We set up a confounder — country group, either high-burden or low-burden — that drives both treatment assignment and outcomes. High-burden countries use CPAP more often and also have lower baseline survival. CPAP itself has a genuine positive effect of about 7 percentage points. *(pause)* When you run the aggregated summary, you'll see CPAP appearing harmful. When you run the disaggregated summary — grouped by country group — you'll see CPAP is beneficial in both strata. The paradox emerges right from the simulation. Play with the probabilities and sample sizes to see how strong the composition effect needs to be to flip the sign.

## Slide: R Block 2: Visualizing Aggregated vs. Stratified
Now we visualize it. Two side-by-side bar charts. On the left, the aggregated view — CPAP in blue, No CPAP in orange. CPAP's bar is shorter. Looks harmful. *(pause)* On the right, the stratified view — same data, broken out by country group. Now CPAP's bar is taller in both panels. Looks beneficial. Same data, opposite conclusions. That subtitle says it all. This is the kind of visualization you'd put in front of a program director to explain why the naive analysis was wrong.

## Slide: R Block 3: Naive vs. Confounder-Adjusted Regression
This is where we connect the paradox to something most of you have already been doing — regression. *(pause)* The naive model regresses survival on CPAP alone. The coefficient will be negative — CPAP appears to hurt survival. That's the Simpson's Paradox showing up in regression form. *(pause)* The adjusted model adds country group as a control variable. Now the CPAP coefficient flips to positive — about 7 percentage points, right where we set it. This is omitted variable bias, which is just the statistical name for what happens when you don't condition on a confounder. The green callout at the bottom sums it up: omitted variable bias is the statistical signature of Simpson's Paradox.

## Slide: R Block 4: Bayesian Network with bnlearn
Now we encode the causal structure directly in a Bayesian network using the bnlearn package. We define the DAG — country group causes both CPAP usage and survival, and CPAP also causes survival. We fit it to the data and run queries. *(pause)* The key thing to notice is that the Bayesian network handles the conditioning correctly by construction. When you query P of survival given CPAP equals Yes, it respects the causal structure encoded in the DAG. You can also condition on specific country groups to see the stratified effects. The BN avoids Simpson's Paradox because it knows about the confounder.

## Slide: R Block 5: Prosecutor's Fallacy Calculator
Last code block. We build a reusable Bayes' rule calculator for diagnostic tests. You feed in sensitivity, specificity, and prevalence, and it spits out the positive predictive value — the probability of disease given a positive test. *(pause)* Run it with our preeclampsia example: 90% sensitivity, 85% specificity, 5% prevalence. PPV is about 24%. Now change the prevalence to 20% — a high-risk population — and PPV jumps to about 60%. Same test, same accuracy, dramatically different meaning. That's the whole point of the Prosecutor's Fallacy lesson. The base rate changes everything.

## Slide: Key Takeaways
Three core lessons to walk away with. *(pause)* First, Simpson's Paradox comes from a confounder fork in the DAG. Aggregated data can reverse the true causal effect. The rule: always stratify by confounders, never by mediators. *(pause)* Second, the Prosecutor's Fallacy confuses P of Evidence given Hypothesis with P of Hypothesis given Evidence. Base rates matter. Always use Bayes' rule to compute the correct posterior. *(pause)* Third, and most practically — in MNH cost-effectiveness, naive cross-country comparisons of CPLS mislead because baseline severity and health system strength confound the treatment-outcome relationship. Causal adjustment doesn't just refine your estimates — it can completely reverse which countries and interventions you prioritize. And that difference can mean thousands of additional lives saved.
