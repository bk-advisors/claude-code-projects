# Speaker Notes — Course Introduction Video

## Opening Hook (30 seconds)

Let me tell you about a decision that nearly cost thousands of newborn lives.

A group of global health donors pooled hundreds of millions of dollars. They had two competing programs — call them Feather Touch and TruSmartz. They ran the regressions. The data said Feather Touch was the clear winner. So they recommended doubling down on it.

The data was wrong. Not because of bad math — because of bad thinking. The analysts confused correlation with causation, and the program they championed was actually riding on factors they never measured.

This course exists so that doesn't happen to you.

*(pause)*

## What This Course Is About (1 minute)

Welcome to Inference and Intervention.

This is a course about one thing: how to think about cause and effect in complex systems — specifically, health systems where millions of dollars and millions of lives are at stake.

Most of you have been trained to analyze data. You can run regressions. You can build dashboards. What you probably haven't been trained to do is think *causally* — to ask not just "what happened?" but "why did it happen?" and, critically, "what would happen if we did something different?"

Those sound like the same question. They are not. And the distance between them is where this entire course lives.

*(pause)*

## The Journey in Four Parts (3 minutes)

*(Reference the whiteboard diagram here — draw the four-phase pathway)*

Let me walk you through what the next ten sessions will look like. Think of it as a journey in four parts.

### Part One: Foundations — Chapters 1 through 3

We start by learning to think causally.

In Chapter 1, I'll show you the three traps that catch even sophisticated analysts — confounding, reverse causation, and selection bias. These aren't rare edge cases. They're the default. Every time you look at data without a causal model, you're walking through a minefield.

In Chapter 2, you'll learn the grammar of causation — the three fundamental structures that govern how information flows through any system. Chains, forks, and colliders. Once you understand these three patterns, you understand the logic of every causal model, no matter how complex.

In Chapter 3, we get practical. You'll build a real causal model for a maternal and newborn health program through iterative interviews — first with ministry officials, then facility directors, then community health workers. Each round reveals what the previous model missed. By the end, your four-node sketch has become a twelve-node map of how the health system actually works.

*(pause)*

### Part Two: Quantify — Chapters 4 through 6

Now we add numbers to the picture.

Chapter 4 introduces probability, Bayes' Rule, and Bayesian networks. You'll learn to put numbers on your causal arrows — not because the numbers will be perfectly right, but because the act of quantifying forces a precision that qualitative reasoning lets you avoid.

Chapter 5 is where the model comes alive. You'll run it forwards — predicting outcomes from causes. And you'll run it backwards — diagnosing causes from outcomes. You'll encounter "explaining away," one of the most counterintuitive phenomena in probabilistic reasoning: confirming one cause of an outcome automatically reduces your belief in alternative causes.

Chapter 6 is the one that will keep you up at night. Simpson's Paradox — the fact that aggregated data can tell you the exact opposite of what disaggregated data shows. I'll show you cases where the "best" program in the aggregate is actually the worst program in every subgroup. And I'll show you why only the causal model can tell you which answer is right.

*(pause)*

### Part Three: Decide — Chapters 7 through 9

This is where the course gets real. You're no longer just analyzing — you're deciding.

Chapter 7 introduces the most important idea in causal inference: the difference between watching and doing. Observing that facilities with CPAP machines have better outcomes is not the same as buying a CPAP machine for a facility that doesn't have one. The do-operator and graph surgery formalize this distinction and let you compute the actual causal effect of an intervention.

Chapter 8 scales up. You have a budget and ten countries. How do you allocate? We'll use portfolio theory — the same framework that revolutionized financial investing — to think about diversification, risk, and sequential decision-making in a health context.

Chapter 9 adds the hardest layer. Other people have strategies too. When you invest in a country, the government responds. Sometimes it co-invests. Sometimes it quietly redirects its budget elsewhere. You'll learn game theory — Nash Equilibrium, the free-rider problem — and, most importantly, how to design contracts that make cooperation rational rather than just hoping for it.

*(pause)*

### Part Four: Discover — Chapter 10

Finally, we close the loop. Can algorithms discover causal structure from data? The answer is: partially. You'll learn what data can tell you, what it fundamentally cannot, and why the most powerful approach combines algorithmic discovery with expert knowledge. Neither alone is enough.

*(pause — draw the feedback arrow on the whiteboard)*

And notice the arrow going back from the end to the beginning. This isn't a one-way trip. Data refines the model. The model guides new data collection. You iterate. That's how understanding grows.

## Who This Course Is For (30 seconds)

This course is for people who will make decisions about health programs — allocating budgets, choosing interventions, evaluating outcomes. You don't need to be a statistician. You don't need to know R, though we'll use it. You need to be willing to draw what you believe on a whiteboard and then be prepared to be wrong.

That willingness — to be explicit about your assumptions and then test them — is the core skill this course teaches.

## What You'll Walk Away With (30 seconds)

By the end of these ten sessions, you'll have:

- A framework for thinking about cause and effect that goes far beyond regression tables
- The ability to build, quantify, and interrogate causal models
- Tools for making decisions under uncertainty — including the uncertainty about how others will respond
- A healthy skepticism about any number that doesn't come with a causal story attached

## Closing (15 seconds)

The gap between good intentions and good outcomes in global health is not a gap of resources or commitment. It's a gap of causal reasoning. This course is designed to close that gap.

I'll see you in Chapter 1.
