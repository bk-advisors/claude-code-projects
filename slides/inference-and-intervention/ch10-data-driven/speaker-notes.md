Third, we'll learn two families of algorithms for discovering DAGs from data: the PC algorithm and hill climbing. ## Slide: Causation vs. Probability
This is a section divider. We're starting with the deepest conceptual question in the chapter — what's the difference between knowing what correlates with what, and knowing what causes what?

## Slide: Two Fundamentally Different Questions
Okay, look at these two columns. On the left, the statistical question: what is the joint distribution of staffing, equipment, and mortality? That's asking about associations — what tends to go with what. On the right, the causal question: if we *increase* staffing, what happens to mortality? That's asking about intervention — what happens when we reach in and change something. *(pause)* Here's what makes this distinction so important. Statistical associations are symmetric — if knowing A tells you something about B, then knowing B tells you something about A, through Bayes' rule. But causation is asymmetric. Causing A affects B, but that doesn't mean causing B affects A. *(pause)* Now look at the red callout. This is the fundamental problem we're going to wrestle with all day: multiple causal structures can produce the *same* joint probability distribution. So you can stare at data all day and not be able to tell which causal story generated it.

## Slide: Three DAGs, One Distribution
Let me make that concrete. We've got three variables: Staffing, Equipment, and Mortality. Look at these three DAGs. The chain — S causes E causes M. The fork — E causes both S and M. The reverse chain — M causes E causes S. Three completely different causal stories. *(pause)* But here's the thing — all three encode the exact same conditional independence: S is independent of M given E. You could collect a million data points and never tell these three apart from observational data alone. *(pause)* The definition box formalizes this. If two DAGs encode the same set of conditional independencies, they're called Markov equivalent — and no amount of data can distinguish them. That's a sobering result.

## Slide: When *Can* Data Distinguish DAGs?
Now here's the good news — and this is really important. Colliders *are* identifiable from data. Look at this structure: S causes E, and M also causes E. That's a collider at E. *(pause)* What makes colliders special? S and M are marginally independent — if you just look at them without conditioning on anything, there's no association. But the moment you condition on E, S and M become dependent. No chain or fork produces that pattern. So if you see this signature in your data — marginal independence that turns into conditional dependence — you've found a collider, and you know the direction of both arrows. This is why colliders are the key to structure learning. They're the one place where data can actually resolve causal direction.

## Slide: Markov Equivalence
Section divider. We're going to formalize what we just saw and understand what it means for working with real program data.

## Slide: Equivalence Classes
A Markov equivalence class is the set of all DAGs that encode the same conditional independencies. We represent it with something called a CPDAG — a Completed Partially Directed Acyclic Graph. In a CPDAG, directed edges appear where *all* DAGs in the class agree on direction — typically around colliders. Undirected edges appear where DAGs disagree. *(pause)* Look at the example on the right with four variables. The data reveals certain independence patterns, and from those we can determine that Quality causes NMR — that edge is directed in every equivalent DAG, so we're confident. But the direction between Staffing and Equipment? That remains ambiguous from data alone. We can see they're connected, but we can't tell which way the arrow points.

## Slide: Implications for MNH Analysis
This is the practical warning, and it's crucial. When we learn a DAG from program data — from DHIS2 — we're learning an equivalence class, not a unique DAG. Some edges will be directed, others won't. *(pause)* So how do we resolve the ambiguity? Four ways. Domain knowledge — experts tell us that training causes competency, not the other way around. Temporal ordering — the past can't be caused by the future. Experimental data — if we have randomized trials, they can identify specific edges. And instrumental variables, which we'll get to later today. *(pause)* The green box at the bottom gives us our strategy: learn the skeleton from the data — which variables are connected — then use domain expertise and temporal ordering to orient the ambiguous edges. Data discovery plus expert knowledge. That's the hybrid approach.

## Slide: Structure Learning
Section divider. Now we get into the algorithmic toolbox — how do we actually discover DAGs from data?

## Slide: Two Approaches to Learning DAGs
Two main families. On the left, constraint-based methods — the PC algorithm. The idea is to test conditional independencies in your data and build the skeleton from the results. You start with every node connected to every other node, then systematically remove edges when you find that two variables are independent. Then you orient edges using collider rules. It's statistically principled and fast for sparse graphs, but it's sensitive to errors in individual independence tests — one bad test can cascade. *(pause)* On the right, score-based methods — hill climbing. The idea here is totally different. You search over the space of possible DAGs, proposing changes — add an edge, remove an edge, reverse an edge — and you keep the change if it improves a scoring function like BIC. It's more robust to individual test errors but can get stuck in local optima. *(pause)* In practice? Many analysts run both and compare results. If both methods agree on an edge, that's reassuring.

## Slide: The PC Algorithm in Detail
Let's walk through the PC algorithm step by step. You start with the complete graph — every node connected. Then you start removing edges. For each pair of variables, you test: are they independent? If yes, remove the edge. You start with marginal independence — conditioning set of size zero — and work your way up to larger conditioning sets. *(pause)* Once you've got the skeleton, you look for v-structures. For every unshielded triple — X connected to Z connected to Y, but X not directly connected to Y — you check: was Z in the separating set when we removed the X-Y edge? If Z was *not* in the separating set, that's a collider, and you orient it as X arrow Z arrow-in Y. *(pause)* Finally, you apply Meek's rules to propagate those orientations to other edges, being careful not to create new colliders or cycles. The output is a CPDAG.

## Slide: Score-Based Learning: BIC
The BIC score — Bayesian Information Criterion — balances two things. The first term measures fit: how well does this DAG explain the data? The second term is a complexity penalty that penalizes extra edges. More edges always improve fit, but the penalty discourages adding edges that don't substantially improve the model. *(pause)* This automatic trade-off between fit and parsimony is what makes BIC so popular. Hill climbing with BIC is the most commonly used structure learning method in practice — it's the `hc()` function in the bnlearn package. Simple to run, and it tends to give sensible results.

## Slide: Assumptions and Limitations
Both methods rest on assumptions, and we need to be honest about them. Causal sufficiency — no hidden confounders. That's almost certainly violated when we're working with program data. We don't observe community-level factors, cultural practices, household income. Faithfulness — no exact cancellation of causal effects. Correct functional form matters for the independence tests. And you need sufficient sample size, especially for constraint-based methods. *(pause)* Now look at that red callout. This is the most important message on this slide: learned DAGs should be treated as *hypotheses*, not ground truth. They're starting points for investigation, not final answers. If you treat a data-mined DAG as gospel, you're making the same mistake as someone who treats a regression coefficient as a causal effect.

## Slide: Instrumental Variables
Section divider. Okay, let's shift gears. What do you do when you've got a confounder you simply can't observe or control for? That's where instrumental variables come in.

## Slide: The Endogeneity Problem
Here's the motivating question: does midwife density cause lower neonatal mortality? Seems straightforward — just regress NMR on midwife density, right? *(pause)* Wrong. Look at the DAG. District wealth is a confounder. Wealthy districts attract more midwives — better salaries, better facilities. And wealthy districts have better outcomes for a dozen other reasons — nutrition, sanitation, access to referral hospitals. So if you run a naive regression of NMR on midwife density, your estimate is biased. You're conflating the causal effect of midwives with the effect of wealth. The red box spells it out: naive regression gives you a biased estimate.

## Slide: The IV Solution
An instrumental variable Z has to satisfy three conditions. Relevance — Z predicts the treatment. Exclusion — Z affects the outcome *only through* the treatment. Independence — Z is unrelated to confounders. *(pause)* Here's our instrument: distance to the nearest training college. Think about it. Districts closer to training colleges have more midwives — that's relevance. The college's physical location doesn't directly cause neonatal deaths — that's exclusion. And college placement was based on historical and political factors that have nothing to do with current district wealth — that's independence. *(pause)* So we can use this geographic quirk to isolate the causal effect of midwife density. We're exploiting a natural experiment.

## Slide: Two-Stage Least Squares (2SLS)
Here's how it works mechanically. Stage 1: regress midwife density on distance to training college. This extracts only the variation in midwife density that's driven by geography — the exogenous part. It purges the variation driven by wealth. Stage 2: regress NMR on the *predicted* midwife density from stage 1. Because we're using only the "clean" variation, the estimate is unbiased. *(pause)* The intuition is powerful. We're comparing districts that differ in midwife density *because of* where training colleges happen to be located — not because of how wealthy they are. It's the closest thing to a randomized experiment you can get without actually running one.

## Slide: IV Assumptions: When They Fail
Each assumption can fail, and each failure has consequences. If distance barely predicts midwife density — weak instrument — your estimates become imprecise and biased toward the OLS estimate. If college towns also happen to have better hospitals — exclusion violation — then your IV estimate captures the hospital effect too, not just midwives. If colleges were historically built in wealthy districts — independence violation — then the IV is confounded just like OLS. *(pause)* Now here's the key asymmetry, in the orange callout. The relevance condition *is* testable — you check the first-stage F-statistic, and the rule of thumb is F greater than 10. But the exclusion restriction? That's *untestable*. It's a domain knowledge argument. You have to convince people that distance to a training college has no direct effect on neonatal mortality. That's a judgment call, not a statistical test.

## Slide: Regression with Causal Awareness
Section divider. Now let's talk about something that trips up analysts every single day — which variables should you control for in a regression, and which ones should you leave alone?

## Slide: Good Controls vs. Bad Controls
This might be one of the most practically important slides in the entire course. Look at the two columns. On the left, good controls: confounders. A confounder is a variable that causes both treatment and outcome. Controlling for it removes the backdoor path and reduces bias. That's what you want. *(pause)* On the right, bad controls: mediators. A mediator is a variable *on* the causal path — treatment causes mediator causes outcome. If you control for the mediator, you block the very causal path you're trying to measure. You end up underestimating the total effect. *(pause)* The take-home: not all control variables improve your estimate. Some make it actively worse. The DAG tells you which is which.

## Slide: The Collider Bias Trap
Here's the really tricky one. Look at this DAG: Staffing causes Quality Rating, and Equipment also causes Quality Rating. Quality Rating is a collider. Now, Staffing and Equipment are independent — neither causes the other. *(pause)* But watch what happens when we condition on Quality Rating. Say we look only at facilities rated "Good." Among those facilities, if staffing is low, they must be compensating with great equipment. If equipment is poor, they must have excellent staffing. We've created a *negative* association between Staffing and Equipment that doesn't exist in reality — it's entirely an artifact of conditioning on the collider. *(pause)* The rule is straightforward: never control for a descendant of the treatment unless you specifically want the controlled direct effect rather than the total effect.

## Slide: Summary: Control Variable Decision Guide
This table should be taped to every program analyst's desk. Five types of variables, five different recommendations. Confounders — yes, control for them, it blocks the backdoor path. Mediators — no, don't control, it blocks the causal path. Colliders — no, don't control, it opens a spurious path. Instruments — use them in 2SLS, not as a regular control. Precision variables — causes of the outcome only — yes, control for them, they reduce variance without introducing bias. *(pause)* And the principle in the definition box is the one sentence I want everyone to remember: use your DAG to determine what to control for. Regression without a causal model is flying blind. You might adjust for something that *increases* bias rather than reducing it.

## Slide: R Workshop: Structure Learning
Section divider. Time to get our hands dirty with code.

## Slide: R Block 1: Simulate DHIS2-Like Data
Alright, here's our first code block. We're simulating a 3,000-facility dataset from a known causal structure. We know the true DAG — we're building it ourselves — so later we can check how well the algorithms recover it. *(pause)* Look at the variable definitions. Training is binary, drug availability is binary, equipment is continuous between 0 and 1. The downstream variables — C-section rate, referral rate, volume, NMR — are all generated as functions of their causal parents, with realistic coefficients and some noise. This gives us DHIS2-like data with a known ground truth. That's the beauty of simulation — you can check your methods against the truth.

## Slide: R Block 2: PC Algorithm — Learn Structure from Data
Now we feed that simulated data to the PC algorithm — no peeking at the true DAG. The `pc.stable` function from bnlearn runs the algorithm, using a mutual information test appropriate for mixed data. *(pause)* Then we compare what it found against the truth. The Structural Hamming Distance — SHD — counts how many edge additions, deletions, or reversals you'd need to get from the learned graph to the true graph. Lower is better. We also count true positives, false positives, and false negatives to get a detailed picture. Run this and see — some edges get recovered perfectly, others get missed or reversed. That's the reality of structure learning.

## Slide: R Block 3: Score-Based Learning (Hill Climbing)
Same data, different algorithm. Now we run hill climbing with BIC scoring. Then we put all three graphs side by side — the true DAG, the PC result, and the hill climbing result. *(pause)* You'll notice the two methods sometimes agree and sometimes disagree. Where they both find an edge and agree on direction, that's strong evidence. Where they disagree, you need to dig deeper. This is why running both approaches is good practice — they have different strengths and weaknesses, and comparing them gives you a more complete picture.

## Slide: Interpreting Structure Learning Results
So what do we typically find when we run these algorithms? The skeleton — which nodes are connected — is usually well-recovered when you have more than about a thousand observations. Edge direction is harder. Many edges remain undirected in the CPDAG. Collider structures are the best-identified patterns, which makes sense given what we discussed earlier. Long chains are the hardest because each intermediate independence test adds error that accumulates. *(pause)* The practical strategy for the MNH program: use the learned skeleton as your starting point, layer in expert orientation to resolve ambiguous directions, and use temporal ordering — things that happened earlier can't be caused by things that happened later. That combination gives you the best final model.

## Slide: R Block 4: Instrumental Variable Estimation
Now we shift to instrumental variables in R. We simulate a scenario where midwife density affects NMR, but district wealth confounds the relationship. Distance to training college serves as our instrument. *(pause)* Then we estimate the causal effect three ways: naive OLS that just regresses NMR on midwife density — this will be biased. Controlled OLS that includes wealth — this is correct *if* you actually observe wealth. And 2SLS using the instrument — this is correct even *without* observing wealth. Compare the three estimates against the true effect of negative 0.4 and you'll see exactly how much damage confounding does, and how the IV recovers the truth.

## Slide: R Block 5: Good vs. Bad Controls in Regression
This last code block demonstrates the two critical mistakes we discussed. First, the mediator trap: Training causes Competency, which causes NMR. If you control for Competency, you block the very path through which Training works, and you massively underestimate Training's total effect. Run it and watch the coefficient shrink. *(pause)* Second, the collider trap: Staffing and Equipment are independent, but both cause Quality. Unconditionally, the correlation between Staffing and Equipment is roughly zero. But condition on high-quality facilities and suddenly you see a negative correlation — the classic collider bias artifact. The plot makes it vivid. These aren't abstract warnings; they're mistakes that happen in real analyses every day.

## Slide: Key Takeaways
Four core lessons to take away from today. First, data alone cannot determine causation. Markov equivalence means multiple DAGs fit the same data, so domain knowledge is essential to resolve the ambiguity. Second, structure learning algorithms discover *candidate* DAGs — the PC algorithm and hill climbing recover the skeleton, and colliders are the key to figuring out which way edges point. Third, instrumental variables give you a way to overcome confounding when you can't control for confounders directly. And fourth — and this is the one I want you to carry into every analysis you do — not all controls are good controls. Controlling for mediators underestimates effects. Controlling for colliders creates bias out of thin air. Let the DAG guide your regression specification.
