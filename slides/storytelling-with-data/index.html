Storytelling with Data

From curiosity to causation — completing the scientific method loop

2026-03-28

The Chart That Started a Project

Last year I read an Our World in Data article about measles vaccination. 94 million lives saved. Powerful number.

But what stopped me wasn’t the number. It was a heatmap of US measles cases, 1929–2022.

  • Before the vaccine: a wall of color. Cases everywhere, every year, every state.
  • After 1963: the color drops off. A cliff. You could see the intervention working.
  • I thought: what does this look like for Africa?

That question led to a project. And it taught me something I keep re-learning: the most important part of a data story is the part that isn’t data. It’s the question that got you to open the dataset.

Part I: Philosophy

Where stories begin

Where It Starts

It all starts with curiosity — that itch of a question you want to answer.

  • A phenomenon you’ve observed in the world
  • A curiosity about what caused it
  • A desire to measure it and estimate what happens next
  • And eventually, a need to communicate it — to get it across to someone who can act on it

Diagnostic (past) → Descriptive (present) → Prescriptive (action) → Predictive (future)

Every data story worth telling has a backward-looking question, a present-tense description, a clear recommendation with a way to communicate it, and a forward-looking implication. Most courses focus on the middle two.

What’s the Norm

The standard analysis process:

Define Problem
→
Collect Data
→
Clean
→
Explore
→
Visualize
→
Report

It’s fine when you’re starting out. But it skips the curiosity, meaning, and creative side. You end up with dashboards nobody reads past the first page. I’ve made plenty of those myself.

  • It tells you how to get from data to chart
  • It doesn’t tell you why you should care, who it’s for, or what you want them to do

Start with an Audience of One

Data visualization is complementary to good writing. The story comes first.

Morgan Housel barely uses charts in his books, but you can’t stop reading. He uses context, comparison, and anecdote to make you care about a number.

Start with one person — a specific reader, colleague, or client. Ask: What would make this person stop scrolling and actually read?

Before you touch data, ask:

  1. Who am I making this for?
  2. Why should it matter to them?
  3. What will move them to act?

Part II: Making a Story

Five steps I keep coming back to

1 Find Your Question

Every data story starts with an itch. Something doesn’t add up. Something surprises you.

Can you fill in this sentence?

“I am curious about X, so I will measure Y to see if Z is true.”

Topic (vague)

“Measles in Africa”

Question (specific)

“Did the 2001 WHO/UNICEF initiative cause the dramatic decline in measles cases across African countries?”

2 Make It Measurable

A question becomes answerable when you can translate it into operations on data.

Vague Specific
“How bad is measles in Africa?” “How did cases per million change across countries, 1980–2023?”
“Should PNG vaccinate for HPV?” “What’s the projected impact on cervical cancer if PNG achieves 80% coverage by 2030?”

Filter (which countries?) → Group (by what?) → Summarize (count, rate, ratio) → Compare (before/after, A vs. B)

3 Analyze

Most findings come from four basic operations:

  1. Filter for extremes or subsets
  2. Group by meaningful categories
  3. Summarize with simple statistics
  4. Compare across groups or time

When analysis yields nothing interesting, don’t reach for fancier tools. Go back and revisit the question. A better question beats a better algorithm. Every time.

4 Synthesize

Declare what you found — in one sentence.

The “so what?” test: If you can’t answer “so what?” in one sentence, you’re still doing exploration, not explanation.

Exploration

“Here are 12 charts showing measles trends by country and year with various normalizations…”

Explanation

“Coordinated campaigns from 2001 drove a 90%+ decline in measles — but a handful of countries still carry most of the burden.”

5 Choose Your Form

The chart type is the last decision, not the first.

  • Change over time? Line chart, heatmap, small multiples
  • Comparing categories? Bar chart, dot plot, table
  • Geographic patterns? Map, cartogram
  • A sequential story? Scrollytelling, annotated walkthrough

The form is the last decision. The question is the point.

A well-chosen chart amplifies a clear finding. No chart can rescue a finding that doesn’t exist.

Part III: The Missing Pieces

Diagnostic and predictive thinking

Most Data Stories Stop Here

The five steps are the prescriptive mode — the how. Combined with description — the what — you can make good charts and clear reports.

But is that enough?

Descriptive + Prescriptive = here’s the data, here’s how to show it well

What I keep finding missing are the two bookends:

  • Diagnostic: Why did this happen? What’s the causal chain?
  • Predictive: What will happen next? What are the scenarios?

The Diagnostic Lens

“Why did this happen?”

The physics side

Every effect has a cause. You don’t just observe that an object accelerated — you identify the force and check whether the model holds. That habit followed me into data work.

The consulting side

Clients don’t want to know what happened. “Measles declined” is interesting. “Measles declined because of coordinated campaigns” is actionable.

The diagnosis is what turns a descriptive briefing into a strategic one.

Africa Measles Heatmap

The descriptive story:

Measles cases dropped 90%+ across Africa.

. . .

The diagnostic story:

The 2001 WHO/UNICEF Measles Initiative marks a visible inflection point. Before 2001: high-burden red tiles everywhere. After 2001: a shift to blues and greens — but not uniformly.

The temporal structure lets you see the intervention effect as a visual cliff. That cliff is the diagnosis.

What the heatmap reveals:

  • Countries with high SIA coverage saw faster declines
  • Countries with conflict or weak health systems lagged
  • The intervention effect is visible as a temporal cliff

The Predictive Lens

“What comes next?”

The physics side

If you understand the mechanism, you can model the future. Newton’s laws don’t just explain why the ball fell — they tell you where it will land.

The consulting side

Recommendations without projected impact are opinions with nice formatting. “80% coverage by 2030 projects a 65% reduction in cervical cancer deaths” — that actually gets funded.

Predictions are what drive decisions. Descriptions just set the scene.

HPV Vaccination in PNG

The recommendation:

Papua New Guinea should implement a national HPV vaccination program.

. . .

The prediction:

PNG has one of the highest cervical cancer rates in the world. Without intervention, the burden grows with the population. With meaningful coverage, the data projects significant reductions.

I chose scrollytelling because the prediction needs to be felt, not just read. As you scroll, the projected future unfolds.

Why scrollytelling for this one:

  • The prediction unfolds as the reader scrolls
  • Different coverage scenarios are explorable
  • It’s one thing to read “65% reduction” — another to watch it happen on screen

Completing the Loop

The full scientific method for data storytelling

Four Modes of Data Storytelling

Diagnostic Why did this happen?
Backward-looking, causal

Descriptive What is happening?
Present state, the data itself

Prescriptive What should we do?
Process and action

Predictive What will happen next?
Forward-looking, modeled

Most frameworks do Descriptive + Prescriptive well. That’s the five-step process — and it’s where I started too.

The best work I’ve seen also engages the bookends: Diagnostic + Predictive.

The Full Loop

  1. What happened — the data, the trend, the chart
  2. Why it happened — the causal story behind the numbers
  3. What we recommend — the action, the intervention
  4. What we expect — the projected impact, the stakes

Most data stories cover one or two of these. The best ones cover all four.

When you cover all four, you’re not presenting data — you’re building an argument. And arguments change minds in ways that dashboards don’t.

The Compound Effect

  • My first data viz in 2015 was a simple bar chart. It wasn’t pretty. But it answered a specific question for a specific person.
  • Each project since has built on the last — the measles heatmap taught me how temporal structure reveals causes, the HPV project taught me how interactivity makes predictions feel real.
  • I didn’t plan this progression. I just kept following questions that interested me.
  • You don’t need to master all four quadrants on day one. But it helps to know they exist, and to notice when your story is missing one.

Start with a clear question. Make it measurable. Sit with the data honestly. Say what you found. Pick a form that serves the finding.

And when you’re ready — ask why it happened and what comes next.
